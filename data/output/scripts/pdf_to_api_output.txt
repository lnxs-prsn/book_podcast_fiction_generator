Priming is saying, "Here’s who I am, here’s what I need, and here’s how I want you to answer." Like when we told GPT, "You are a helpful assistant. Provide short answers in Markdown." Suddenly, it wasn’t a genius—it was a *tool*.

**RAG AND LANGCHAIN**  
HOST: But here’s the thing: even the smartest LLM is frozen in time. It doesn’t know your company’s latest Q3 report. Or your patient’s history from last month. How do you fix that?  
EXPERT: You give it *memory*. Not memorization—*context*. That’s Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG. Imagine you’re a doctor: "Who was that patient who complained about ear pain and had migraines?" The LLM can’t answer. Unless...  

HOST: Unless you feed it something?  
EXPERT: Exactly. RAG takes your documents—medical notes, PDFs, anything—slices them into chunks, and turns each chunk into numbers. Embeddings. They’re like fingerprints of meaning. Then it stores these in a vector database—think of it as a hyper-smart filing system. When you ask a question, it finds the most relevant chunks and hands them to the LLM with your query.  

HOST: So the LLM isn’t answering from thin air? It’s reading your world?  
EXPERT: Precisely. And LangChain ["lang-chayn"] is the orchestra conductor. It stitches together the embeddings, the database, and the LLM into one flow. It’s not just code—it’s the blueprint for making a machine *understand* your world.  

**LANGCHAIN IN ACTION**  
HOST: That doctor example... let’s go back. The one who forgot a patient.  
EXPERT: Right. The tool we built with LangChain? It searches through thousands of notes in seconds. Embeddings turn "ear pain and migraines" into a vector. The vector database finds notes about ear pain and migraines. LangChain feeds the original note to the LLM, which says, "That’s Patient 742. Seen in May." It’s not magic—it’s giving the doctor back their memory.
