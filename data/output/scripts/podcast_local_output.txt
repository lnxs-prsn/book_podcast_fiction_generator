**[6:00-10:00] Prompt Engineering**  
HOST: Speaking of shaping behavior, let’s talk prompt engineering. What’s the deal with "priming"?  
EXPERT: Priming is like giving a model a role and context before you ask it to do anything. It’s how you control its tone, style, and rules. You do this with two types of prompts: the *system* prompt sets the rules, and the *user* prompt gives examples.  
HOST: Can you give an example?  
EXPERT: Sure! Say you want concise answers in Markdown. Your system prompt could be: "You’re a helpful assistant. Give short answers in Markdown." Then you prime it with a user prompt like, "How do I import pandas?" and show the ideal assistant response: "Use this: `import pandas as pd`." Now, when you ask, "How do I import numpy?" it mirrors that format.  
HOST: So it learns from examples?  
EXPERT: Exactly! This is critical for consistency. Priming turns a general-purpose model into a specialized tool. Without it, you’d get random styles or errors. The catch? It takes trial-and-error to get the prompts just right.

**[15:00-21:00] RAG & LangChain**  
HOST: Alright, here’s the big one: Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG. What is it?  
EXPERT: RAG solves a major LLM flaw: they can’t access up-to-date or specialized knowledge. Imagine an LLM analyzing your company’s entire PDF library to answer questions. RAG bridges that gap by combining LLMs with your data.  
HOST: How does that work technically?  
EXPERT: First, you chop your documents into chunks. Each chunk gets converted into numerical "embeddings"—think of them as AI fingerprints. You store these in a vector database like FAISS, pronounced "fayce," which acts as a smart index. When a user asks a question, it gets converted to an embedding too, and the database finds the most similar chunks.  
HOST: Then what?  
EXPERT: The LLM uses those chunks as context to answer. For example, if you ask, "Which companies paid dividends in 2023?" it might grab a chunk about "fiscal 2023 cash dividends" and build a precise answer. This avoids hallucinations and ensures accuracy.  
HOST: What about chunking? How big should those be?  
EXPERT: Chunk size depends on your model’s context window. Too small, and you lose meaning; too big, and you overload the model. The key is balancing detail and efficiency. LangChain simplifies this by handling the pipeline—connecting data sources, chunking, and similarity search.  

**[21:00-24:00] LangChain in Action**  
HOST: Let’s make this concrete. The PDF mentioned a hospital example.  
EXPERT: Perfect! Imagine a doctor needing to find patients with ear pain and a migraine history. LangChain ingests all medical notes, chunks them, embeds them, and stores them in a vector database. When the doctor asks, "Who had ear pain and migraines?" LangChain:  
1. Embeds the query.  
2. Finds matching notes.  
3. Feeds them to the LLM with context.  
HOST: So it’s like a super-powered search?  
EXPERT: Exactly! But here’s the catch: similarity search alone isn’t foolproof. If a query is "Who had ear pain in March?" but a note says "ear pain in April," it might miss the match. That’s where LLM judgment comes in—it reviews search results and corrects errors.
