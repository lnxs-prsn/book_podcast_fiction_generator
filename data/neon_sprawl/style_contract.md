## DOCUMENT 3: STYLE CONTRACT

### Voice

- **Sensory Grounding.** Every technical event registers first as sensation — heat behind the eyes, pressure in the temples, the taste of copper when the implant overloads — before any label appears. — Not: "Lyra experienced a buffer overflow." Instead: "Hex strobed behind her eyelids, each flash a packet she couldn't swallow. Her temples throbbed with the rhythm of dropped frames."
- **Street Logic over Sysadmin Speak.** Characters use the Sprawl's working slang ("choke," "flush," "ghost," "dirty feed") not academic terms. The technical label arrives only *after* the reader has lived the mechanic. — Not: "She implemented a stop-word filter." Instead: "She taught her implant to choke on the beacon-spam, let the weather through. Took three tries. The third try didn't kill her."
- **Failure as Physical Cost.** Every mistake extracts something bodily — calories, memory, biometric access, a friend's life. No abstract "loss of reputation." — Not: "Her reputation suffered." Instead: "The dispenser read her palm and stayed dark. Zero balance. She'd burned three days of cycles on a poisoned token."
- **No Expositionary Dialogue.** Mentors don't lecture. They hand Lyra a broken tool, let her cut herself, then show the scar pattern. — Not: "Remember, pipelines are atomic," Kael said. Instead: Kael tossed her a cortex dump. "This one rolled back halfway. Read it. Tell me what's missing."

### Jargon Introduction Rule

1. **Experience the problem** — protagonist encounters it physically or practically before any label exists
2. **Feel the stakes** — something goes wrong, or nearly goes wrong, because of it
3. **Receive the pattern** — a mentor, text, or observation names the underlying structure
4. **Attach the label** — the technical term appears *only after* the reader already grasps the concept
5. **Use naturally thereafter** — no scare quotes, no re-explanation in later chapters

### Failure Dramatization

- **Immediate physical/practical consequence.** The implant seizes, the dispenser locks, the Ledger publishes her root key, a friend dies in the turf war her lemma collision triggered. Failure is *concrete* — a body, a door that won't open, a memory gone.
- **Emotional cost.** Shame that she poisoned her crew's corpus. Grief that her normalization killed people she knew. Terror that her biometric root is public. Not just pain — the specific wound of *her choice* causing *their harm*.
- **Social stakes.** CorpSec drones tag her as malformed traffic. Rival crews read her exposed logs and hunt her mentor. The Ledger's public leaderboard displays her failed commit for every scraper in the Sprawl. She is judged by the old rules (loyalty, territory, blood) and the new rules (cycle quota, vocab compliance, provenance) simultaneously.

### What Never Happens

- A character lectures another using jargon they both just learned
- A technique/skill/action succeeds perfectly the first time (no learning)
- Lyra accesses a technical term in her internal monologue before earning it through failure
- The Sprawl's systems behave deterministically for narrative convenience — drift, eviction, and OOV rejection follow their own logic, not plot needs
- A mentor "gives" Lyra the answer; they only ever give her the *broken tool* and wait
