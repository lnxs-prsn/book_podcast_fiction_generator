Speaker 1: Then start with the problem. Large language models are brilliant but frozen in time. Their training data has a cutoff. They do not know what happened yesterday. Speaker 0: That is a real problem.

Speaker 1: That is the problem RAG solves. Retrieval-augmented generation. Here is how it works in plain human experience. You have a bunch of documents. Medical notes. Product specs. Customer support tickets. Whatever. You chunk those documents into small pieces, a few paragraphs each. You send each chunk through an embedding model. They call it an embedding — which is just a way of pressing the shape of meaning into numbers so a machine can compare ideas the way you compare feelings. Two chunks that mean similar things will have numbers that are close together. That is the retrieval part. When a user asks a question, you embed the question the same way, then search for the chunks whose numbers are closest to the question's numbers. You take those chunks. You paste them into a prompt. You say to the model — here is some context, answer based on this. That is RAG. You are not teaching the model anything new permanently. You are handing it notes before the exam.
