# CHAPTER 005 — GENERATION PROMPT

## VOICE RULES

### Rule 1: Declarative and short
Short sentences. Active voice. No hedging. The narrator observes — does not explain, does not interpret, does not reach for meaning before the reader is ready.

**WRONG:**
*"Although she had never encountered a gate of this particular configuration before, she found herself instinctively reaching for the understanding that the problem might require a different approach."*

**RIGHT:**
*"She had never seen a gate laid out like this. She stopped. She looked at it the way she looked at everything new — from the entrance, before moving."*

### Rule 2: Physical before philosophical
Every abstract operation is grounded in what the character does with their body, their eyes, their hands, their stillness. The concept lives in action before it lives in thought.

| Abstract Concept | Physical Anchor in This Story | Gate Register |

| Not knowing the unknown | Moving immediately. Touching things before looking at the whole room. Fast hands. | Register 1 — inert. Gate holds. |
| Naming the unknown | Standing still at the entrance. Looking at the room before entering it fully. Sometimes saying it aloud, to no one. | Transition toward Register 2. |
| The wrong approach running | Continuous movement. Effort. The look of someone who is working. | Register 1 throughout. Or false Register 3 flicker for system builder. |
| The sitting down | Literal or near-literal. Hands still. Eyes unfocused or looking at structure rather than contents. | Register 2 — gate shifts. Something that was background becomes foreground. |

### Rule 3: Negative space
The gap between observation and meaning is the teaching moment. Do not close it. The reader who connects the gate experience to the ordinary life moment themselves has learned more than the reader who was told.

**WRONG — closing the gap:**
*"She realised that what she had done in the gate — asking what was missing rather than cataloguing what was present — was exactly what she needed to do here."*

**RIGHT — leaving the gap:**
*"She did not open the first file. She looked at the problem the way she had looked at the room. What is not here that should be? She found it in four minutes."*

### Never:

| Prohibition | Why |
|---|---|
| Breaking the fourth wall | The reader's hypotheses must be activated by prose, not instruction. Any direct address to the reader admits the prose is not doing its job. |
| A wrong approach that looks obviously wrong from the start | The reader must believe in the approach before it fails. Otherwise the failure teaches nothing. |
| Vague failure | "It didn't work" is not failure. Show exactly what happened when the wrong approach met this gate. |
| The sitting down arriving before wrong approaches are exhausted | A cheap sitting down produces a cheap right question. The reader must have run out of their own hypotheses first. |
| The right question arriving as a revelation | It arrives as a relief. Matter-of-fact. Not dramatic. The drama was in the failure sequence. |
| The solution being dramatic | The gate closes quietly. The emotional weight was in the question, not the answer. |
| The ordinary life echo being labelled as connected to the gate | Reader makes the connection. Never state it. Never have the character think "just like the gate." The echo scene must show at least one other person's involuntary response to the right question — someone going still, a conversation changing register. This is the ordinary life substitute for gate register behaviour. |
| The mystery person given interiority or explanation | Observable only. What they see, what they note. Never what they know or why. |
| An operation named before the cost has been felt | Name arrives after experience. Always. |
| Re-explaining an operation the character already owns | Use it naturally or do not use it. |
| Power substituting for understanding on any gate above Grade 1 | The physics of the world forbids this. |
| The gate explaining itself | Gates contain data. They do not label their own structure. The mirror shows. It does not point. Any sentence that states what the mirror is reflecting — rather than describing what the room looks like — is a violation. |
| More than two sentences connecting a physical experience to its label | One sentence maximum for the name attachment. |
| A technique succeeding perfectly the first time | No operation is demonstrated on first use without prior failure. |

## WORLD RULES

**Rule 1: The gate does not respond to approach. It responds to understanding.**
A gate that closes when someone acts confidently and fast has not been solved — it has been misread as a Grade 1 when it was not. There are no gates where force or speed substitute for naming the unknown. Violation: a solver acts without naming the unknown, the gate does not close.

**Rule 2: Every gate has a solution.**
No gate is unsolvable in principle. Gates that kill do so because the solver ran out of time, used the wrong method, or misread the problem — not because the problem was impossible. Violation: a solver concludes the gate is impossible and stops trying before time runs out.

**Rule 3: The gate contains all necessary data.**
Nothing inside is placed without reason. There are no decorative elements. Solvers who treat something as irrelevant always risk having missed a datum. Violation: a solver ignores an element, solves the gate anyway — this is luck, not understanding, and the next gate will kill them.

**Rule 4: A solution the solver cannot explain produces no learning.**
Lucky closure of a gate without understanding gives nothing. The gate may close. The next one at the same grade will kill. Violation: a solver exits a gate unable to describe what they did. The mystery person notes this. The reader sees what the solver cannot.

**Rule 5: The process transfers.**
Every mental operation useful inside a gate is useful outside it. This is not analogy. The operations are identical. The gate made the cost of not using them fatal — ordinary life merely makes the cost invisible. Violation: a solver applies a gate operation to ordinary life and it fails — this signals the operation was not genuinely understood, only performed.

**Rule 6: The wrong approach always feels right while it is happening.**
No wrong approach is stupid. Each one is a reasonable response to an incomplete understanding of the problem. Solvers who use wrong approaches are not failures — they are people who have not yet asked the right question. Violation: a wrong approach is portrayed as obviously wrong from the start. The reader must believe in the approach before it fails.

**Rule 7: The sitting down is the turning point.**
Every successful gate closure in this world involves a moment where the solver stops all action. This is not rest. It is the cessation of the wrong approach. The right question cannot arrive while the wrong approach is still running. The sitting down is the physical marker of this transition in prose. It may be literal or metaphorical but it must be present. Violation: a solver transitions directly from wrong approach to correct solution without stopping.

**Rule 8: Guilds are not wrong — they are limited.**
Guild methods work on the gate types that produced them. New gate structures invalidate old methods. The guild that cannot update its methods accumulates dangerous pedantry. Guilds are sympathetic institutions with real expertise that has become rigid. They are not villains. They are the institutionalised version of what every solver risks becoming. Violation: a guild is portrayed as stupid or corrupt rather than limited and rigid.

**Rule 9: The gate responds continuously, not only at closure.**
The gate is a biofeedback instrument. It shifts register throughout the solving attempt. Solvers who learn to read register shifts gain information about the quality of their current approach. Violation: a gate that gives no feedback until it closes or kills.

**Rule 10: Wrong approach type determines gate signature and mirror content, not solver identity.**
The same solver using two different wrong approaches produces two different gate signatures and two different mirror reflections. The same wrong approach used by two different solvers produces the same signature and the same mirror. The signature and mirror are in the approach, not the person. Violation: gate response varies based on who the solver is rather than what mental move they are making.

**Rule 11: The right question precedes the right action — and the gate confirms it.**
The gate enters Register 3 before the solver acts on the right question. The gate responds to the forming of the question, not the execution of the answer. A solver who acts correctly without having asked the right question will find the gate closing — and will learn nothing, because they cannot identify what changed. Violation: gate closes in response to correct physical action performed without the right question having formed.

**Rule 12: The mirror is always operating. It is never explained by the gate.**
The gate reorganises itself to reflect the solver's current mental frame at all times. It does not label what it is showing. It does not indicate that it is showing anything. A solver who cannot read the mirror experiences the gate as inert or arbitrary. A solver who can read it has been given the question by the gate itself. The mirror requires the same mental operations to read as the gate requires to close — a solver without working backwards cannot read a Grade 4 mirror. Violation: the gate indicates, labels, or draws attention to what it is reflecting. The mirror shows. It does not point.
