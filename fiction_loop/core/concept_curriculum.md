*The teaching plan. Maps which Pólya operation gets taught in which arc, through which gate events and ordinary-life moments, and in which order.*

*Agent instruction: Before assembling any chapter prompt, check this document for the current arc's planned operations and the spaced repetition map. Do not introduce an operation before its planned chapter. Do not skip the wrong approach sequence.*

## 1. TEACHING PRINCIPLES

| Principle | What It Means | What Violates It |
|---|---|---|
| Productive failure before understanding | The reader must fail alongside the characters before the right question arrives. Multiple wrong approaches must run their full course first. | Showing the right approach before the wrong approaches have been exhausted. |
| Concrete before abstract | Every operation arrives as a gate experience first, a name second. The name arrives after the felt knowledge, not before. | Naming an operation before the reader has felt the cost of not having it. |
| Wrong approaches feel right | Each wrong approach must be portrayed as reasonable. The reader must believe in it. Only then does its failure teach. | Making the wrong approach obviously wrong from the start. |
| Failure teaches specifically | The failure of each wrong approach must be physically specific. Not "it didn't work" but exactly what happened when it didn't. | Vague failure. The reader must see the exact mechanism of each failure. |
| One hard operation per arc | Easy operations can pair with hard ones. Never two hard operations in the same arc. | Introducing working backwards and restatement in the same arc. |
| Repetition without re-teaching | Once an operation is earned, later chapters use it naturally. No re-explanation. | Re-defining an operation a character already owns. |
| Transfer is the proof | Every arc must show the operation outside the gate as well as inside. Invisible transfer — reader makes the connection without being told. | Explaining that the character is applying the gate method to ordinary life. |
| The sitting down must be earned | The transition from wrong approach to right question must be preceded by exhaustion of wrong approaches. A sitting down that arrives early is cheap. | Solver sits down and finds the answer after one wrong attempt. |
| The fourth wall never breaks | The reader's wrong hypotheses must be activated by the prose itself. Never address the reader directly. Never instruct them to think or guess. | Any sentence that addresses the reader as "you" or instructs them to pause. |
| Mobilisation before organisation | Knowledge must first be recalled (mobilisation) before it can be combined (organisation). A solver who skips to organisation without mobilisation produces a brittle solution. | A character combining elements they never visibly recalled or searched for. |
| Heuristic is not proof | Provisional reasoning that finds the path is not the same as rigorous verification that the path is correct. Both are needed. Neither replaces the other. | Treating a plausible guess as confirmed truth, or refusing to guess because it is not yet proven. |

## 2. DIFFICULTY SCALE

| Difficulty | Description | Pólya Operations at This Level |
|---|---|---|
| 2-3 Easy | Intuitive from daily life. Reader already half-knows it. | Identify unknown / data / condition. Check the result. Did you use all the data? Separate parts of condition. |
| 4-5 Medium | Requires holding two processes in mind simultaneously. | Look at the unknown. Recall related problem. Analogy (simpler analogous problem). Decompose condition. Auxiliary elements. Looking back / transfer. |
| 6-7 Hard | Counter-intuitive. Challenges how the character understands problems themselves. | Auxiliary problem (deliberately invented). Working backwards. Variation / restatement. Generalisation. Specialisation. |
| 7 Paradox | Specifically counter-intuitive in a structural way — the move that looks wrong is right. | Inventor's Paradox (the more ambitious problem is easier). Reductio ad absurdum. |
| 8 Meta | Only accessible after all prior operations are earned. Requires reasoning about reasoning. | Heuristic vs proof distinction. Auxiliary problem chains. Subconscious work. Full integration. |

## 3. FULL PÓLYA OPERATION LIST WITH RATINGS

*Source: Pólya, How to Solve It, Part III (Short Dictionary of Heuristic) and Part I (In the Classroom). Operations below are drawn from the primary text, not from secondary interpretation. Each is a distinct mental move, not a variant of another.*

| Operation | Difficulty | What It Is | What Failing It Looks Like | Arc Introduced |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| Specialisation | 5 | Passing from a general case to a specific one, to find a foothold, a simpler version that can be solved and used as a stepping stone. | Insisting on solving the general case. Refusing to try simpler versions that would have shown the method. | Arc 4 |
| Working backwards / analysis | 7 | Assuming the solution exists and asking what must be true if it does. Chaining back from the desired end to known ground. Reverses direction of reasoning. | Reasoning only forward from data. Every dead end treated as a failure, not as information about what the end cannot look like. | Arc 5 |
| Variation of the problem / restatement | 7 | Questioning whether the stated problem is the real problem. Restating the condition in equivalent or differently-weighted form to find a more accessible version. | Solving the stated problem correctly and stopping. Never asking whether the stated problem was the right problem. | Arc 6 |
| Inventor's Paradox | 7 | The counter-intuitive move: the more ambitious, more general problem is often easier because it reveals the underlying structure the specific problem hides. | Restricting to the specific problem out of caution. The very specificity that seems to make it manageable is what makes it hard. | Arc 5 |
| Reductio ad absurdum | 7 | Assuming the opposite of what you want to prove, deriving a manifest contradiction, and concluding the original assertion must be true. | Requiring direct proof before attempting any argument. Refusing to start from an assumption that feels false. | Arc 5 |
| Heuristic reasoning vs proof distinction | 8 | Understanding that provisional, plausible reasoning (finding the path) is legitimate and necessary, but distinct from verification (proving the path is correct). Using both consciously. | Refusing to commit to any path without proof first. Or: treating heuristic confidence as proven fact. Both are errors. | Arc 7 |
| Subconscious work | 8 | The phenomenon in which stopping all active effort on a problem — rest, unrelated activity — allows the right question to surface. The sitting down is the physical marker. Subconscious work is why it works. | Forcing. Continuing to push when the wrong approaches are exhausted, as if more effort will substitute for the right question. | Arc 7 |
| Auxiliary problem chains | 8 | Constructing not one but a sequence of equivalent auxiliary problems, each reducing to the next, until reaching one that is directly solvable. Then synthesising back. | Treating each auxiliary problem as final. No chain. Each invented problem attempted directly rather than used as a step. | Arc 7 |

## 6. CHAPTER-LEVEL STRUCTURE

*Every chapter follows this sequence. Variation is in content, not structure.*

| Phase | What Happens | Length | Agent Instruction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gate opens | Setting established. Problem space described with enough specificity to activate reader's hypothesis. | Short. One to three paragraphs. | Describe the gate interior precisely. Unknown, data, condition must all be present but not labelled. |
| Wrong approach 1 | First solver acts on obvious hypothesis. Full sequence shown. Gate does not respond. Physical consequence specific. | Medium. The reader's first hypothesis fails here. | Select from wrong approach types not yet used in this context. Show the approach feeling right before it fails. |
| Wrong approach 2 | Second solver with different wrong approach. Gate does not respond. Something small noticed that does not fit. | Medium. The reader's adjusted hypothesis fails here. | Must be visibly different from wrong approach 1. The small noticed thing is the seed of the right question. |
| The sitting down | Solver stops all action. The pause. No new approach yet. Just the absence of the wrong approaches. | Short. One to three paragraphs. | Physical. Literal or near-literal. Hands still. Looking at the room differently. Not at the contents — at the structure. |
| The right question | Arrives quietly. Not as a revelation. As a relief. The answer follows almost immediately. | Short. The solution is anticlimactic after the question. | The question is simple. One sentence. The answer to the question produces the solution in seconds or minutes. |
| Gate closes | Physical. Matter-of-fact. The gate closes. People who were lost inside come out. | Very short. | No celebration. No explanation. The gate closes the way a problem resolves when the right answer is given. |
| Looking back | Solver stays after others leave. Cannot fully explain what they did. Files it. | Short. Private. | Optional name attached here by narrator — one word or phrase maximum, only if naturally arising. Never a lecture. |
| Ordinary life echo | Days or weeks later. Same question applied to ordinary problem. Never labelled as connected to the gate. | Medium. Full scene. | Reader makes the connection. Never stated. The solver does not know they are applying the gate method. At least one other person in the scene goes still or conversation changes register when the right question forms. |

*Note for Arcs 4+: The chapter structure may expand to include the auxiliary problem sub-sequence: wrong approach → auxiliary problem invented → auxiliary solved → synthesis back to original. This is a structural extension of the sitting down phase, not a replacement for it.*

## 8. OPERATIONS THE ORIGINAL CURRICULUM MISSED — NOTES FOR WRITERS

*These are the operations that were absent from the first curriculum draft. Writers and agents must treat them as first-class operations, not as variants of existing ones.*

**Analogy (simpler analogous problem)** is not the same as recalling a related problem. Recall is memory. Analogy is construction. The solver who uses analogy invents a simpler problem with identical structural relations and solves it first — using the method, the result, or both. The gate reward for analogy is distinct: the gate responds to the structural resemblance the solver has found, not to the direct solution attempt. Mirror content for analogy: the gate room reorganises to show the analogous structure alongside the original structure. The solver who can read the mirror sees both rooms at once.

**Auxiliary problem (invented)** is not recall and not analogy. It is the deliberate construction of a different problem as a stepping stone, knowing that the stepping stone may consume time and may not succeed. The risk is explicit — Pólya names it. The solver is not certain the auxiliary will help; they choose it because the original path is exhausted and the auxiliary has some chance. Gate signature: Register 2 on the auxiliary problem itself, not on the original gate. The original gate holds at Register 1 until the auxiliary yields something usable.

**Inventor's Paradox** must be shown, not stated. The moment arrives when a solver makes the problem bigger — more general, more ambitious — and the gate responds where the specific version could not. The reader must experience this as a surprise. A solver who kept the problem small fails. The solver who expanded it closes the gate. The paradox is legible only in retrospect.

**Generalisation and Specialisation** are opposite moves on the same axis. Generalisation expands scope to find structure. Specialisation contracts scope to find a foothold. They often work together: specialise to find the simplest case that shows the pattern, generalise from that pattern to the full solution. Each is a wrong approach when used mechanically — the specialiser who finds one case and stops, the generaliser who loses the specific problem entirely.

**Reductio ad absurdum** is a reasoning move, not a gate type. It appears when a solver needs to prove something is impossible or necessary. The gate shows this as: the solver assumes the opposite of what they want, follows the consequences until something breaks, and the break is the proof. Mirror content: the room shows the impossible situation in detail — and then shows the contradiction. The solver who can read this mirror sees the proof before they articulate it.

**Subconscious work** is the explanation for why the sitting down works. By Arc 7 it should be named explicitly — characters have noticed that the right question arrives after they stop, not while they push. The sitting down is the physical marker. Subconscious work is the mechanism. A character naming this — tentatively, not as theory — is the 1N touch.

**Auxiliary problem chains (Pappus synthesis)** is the late-game operation. Analysis builds the chain forward from the proposed problem to something solvable. Synthesis walks back through the chain to the original solution. Most solvers who use chains do the analysis well but fail the synthesis — they walk forward to the solvable problem and stop, not realising they must retrace to construct the final answer. Gate signature for failed synthesis: Register 3 on the chain endpoint, Register 1 on the original gate — because the chain was built in the wrong direction.

## 9. READER EXPERTISE PROGRESSION

*The reader's growing expertise changes how chapters should be written. Early chapters need full wrong approach sequences. Later chapters can be more implicit.*

| Arc | Reader's State | Chapter Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | No prior knowledge of the process. Fully inside the wrong approaches. | Full productive failure sequence every chapter. Three wrong approaches minimum. |
| 3-4 | Beginning to recognise the wrong approach pattern. Still surprised by specific varieties. Analogy and auxiliary problem are new — reader experiences them alongside the character. | Two wrong approaches shown fully. Third may be shortened. Reader is ahead of character on familiar wrong approaches but behind on the new operations. |
