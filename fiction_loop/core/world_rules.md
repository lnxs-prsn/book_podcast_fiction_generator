## 1. PREMISE

African gate-clearing fiction in the Korean/Japanese tradition: dimensional gates open without warning on Earth, pulling in whoever is nearby. Those inside must solve their way out. This story transposes that genre into contemporary Africa — African cities, African characters, African institutions.

| Field | Answer |
|---|---|
| Core equation | The gate IS a problem. Pólya's process IS the only reliable method for closing it. Not a metaphor. The gate's structure is a problem's structure: unknown, data, condition. Its failure modes are a problem's failure modes. |
| Target reader | Anyone who has ever faced a problem they did not know how to approach. No prior knowledge of Pólya or gate fiction required. |
| Teaching goal | By the end, the reader has absorbed Pólya's mental operations as instinct, not as a list. They have experienced the operations failing and arriving across enough different human contexts that they apply them without consciously naming them. |
| What makes this different from thing fiction | In thing fiction the concept IS the world's physics — the reader absorbs it by living inside the world. In process fiction the process must be experienced, not observed. The reader must undergo the failure of not having the process before the process arrives. Demonstration is not enough. |

## 2. THE PROGRESSION STAGES

*Gate grade IS the progression system. Each grade requires a mental operation the previous grade did not demand. Grades are not strictly sequential in narrative — a character may encounter a Grade 3 gate before fully owning Grade 2 operations. What matters is that the gate punishes the missing operation specifically.*

| Grade | Problem Structure | Operations Required | Core Truth | Failure Mode | Prior Grade's Limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | One unknown, data obvious, condition explicit. | Name the unknown before acting. Separate parts of condition. Find what is missing. | Most problems fail at the first step — before any method is applied. | Rushing to action without naming what is wanted. Cataloguing what is present instead of asking what is absent. | Entry point. |
| 2 | Unknown clear but connection to data non-obvious. Multiple data elements, one unused. | Look at the unknown. Do you know a related problem? Did you use all the data? | Every problem has ancestors. The unused datum is the gate. | No prior knowledge mobilised. Only direct approach. One element of data consistently ignored. | Direct action finds no path. Looking at data rather than unknown. |
| 3 | Condition has multiple parts. Full condition unsolvable directly. Analogous simpler structure exists. | Decompose condition / keep part drop part. Analogy — simpler analogous problem. Auxiliary elements. Can you derive the result differently? | A problem you cannot solve whole you may solve in parts. A simpler problem with the same structure can show the method. | Treating full condition as single unit. Paralysis. Never asking whether a simpler version exists with identical relations. | Recalled problem does not match full condition. No bridge between the parts found by decomposition. |
| 4 | No directly accessible foothold. Forward path exhausted within reach of known methods. | Auxiliary problem (invented stepping stone). Specialisation. Can you use the result? | The direct path is not always the right path. A detour invented for purpose is a legitimate move. | Pushing harder on exhausted forward path. Stopping at domain boundary. Never inventing a detour — only trying to recall one. | Decomposition produces parts with no connecting structure. Analogous problem does not exist in memory. |
| 5 | Unknown not reachable from data side. Forward reasoning dead-ends. Problem may be bigger than stated. | Working backwards / analysis. Generalisation. Inventor's Paradox. Reductio ad absurdum. | Start from the end. The more ambitious problem may be easier. Assuming the opposite to find the contradiction. | Forward reasoning only. Refusing to assume what is not yet proven. Restricting to specific case when general structure would have opened it. | Invented stepping stone reaches a point with no forward connection to the unknown. |
| 6 | Problem as stated is wrong. Valid solution produces no gate closure. | Variation / restatement. Correctly stating the problem before solving it. | The most dangerous gate looks solvable but isn't — as stated. | Solving the stated problem correctly and stopping. Testing variations of the wrong statement. | Backwards chain from wrong statement produces confident wrong answer. |
| 7 | Method found but cannot be explained or verified. Or: method works but cannot be distinguished from luck. | Heuristic vs proof distinction. Subconscious work. | Finding the path is not verifying the path. Both are required. Stopping all effort is sometimes the necessary step. | Treating heuristic confidence as proof. Or: refusing to act without proof first. Forcing when the wrong approaches are exhausted. | Correct restatement produces a problem the solver can follow heuristically but cannot verify. |
| 8 | Multiple equivalent reductions required. No single step sufficient. Solution requires constructing and reversing a chain. | Auxiliary problem chains. Pappus synthesis — analysis forward, synthesis back. | The solution is constructed step by step and verified by retracing. | Treating the chain endpoint as the answer. Building the chain forward without knowing how to reverse it. | Single auxiliary problem solves a sub-problem but leaves the original gate unclosed. |

## 3. THE WORLD'S GOVERNING FORCES

**Event source:** Gates. They open without warning, in any location, pulling in whoever is proximate. No known pattern of selection. No known sender. Frequency increasing. Society has adapted — gate response infrastructure exists — but gates remain fundamentally unexplained.

**Storage layer:** Human knowledge and habit. What solvers learn from gates persists only in memory, reflection, and teaching. The guild system attempts to codify this. Most codification produces pedantry. Genuine learning is individual and slow.

**The protagonist role:** Anyone pulled into a gate becomes a problem-solver by necessity. The question is how they solve and what they carry out. The story itself has NO single protagonist — each gate has its focal solver, but the book is an ensemble (owner decision D6). No recurring character may be promoted into a through-line protagonist; the through-line belongs to the process and the mystery person's thread.

**Tribulation equivalent:** A gate whose grade exceeds the solver's current range of mental operations. Not because it is more powerful — because it requires a move the solver has never made.

**The macro mystery:** Why do gates exist? Who or what sends them? The response infrastructure treats this as unsolvable and focuses on gate-clearing. The mystery person appears to be investigating the macro problem. The reader accumulates evidence alongside them across many arcs. The macro mystery is itself a Pólya problem — and the institutions are solving the wrong stated version of it.

The deeper question — which the mystery person alone is close to asking correctly — is not why gates exist but what they are saying. Gates mirror solver process back at solvers with increasing precision. Across thousands of gates and thousands of solvers, a pattern is emerging in what the gates keep trying to show. The mystery person's notebook is, without their fully knowing it, a record of this communication. The macro mystery restated correctly: the gates are not tests. They are a message. The message is the process itself.

## 4. WORLD RULES
*Each rule can be violated in a scene that produces a physical consequence. If a rule cannot be violated, it is not a story rule — it is an explanation.*

**Rule 1: The gate does not respond to approach. It responds to understanding.**
A gate that closes when someone acts confidently and fast has not been solved — it has been misread as a Grade 1 when it was not. There are no gates where force or speed substitute for naming the unknown. Violation: a solver acts without naming the unknown, the gate does not close.

**Rule 2: Every gate has a solution.**
No gate is unsolvable in principle. Gates that kill do so because the solver ran out of time, used the wrong method, or misread the problem — not because the problem was impossible. Violation: a solver concludes the gate is impossible and stops trying before time runs out.

**Rule 3: The gate contains all necessary data.**
Nothing inside is placed without reason. There are no decorative elements. Solvers who treat something as irrelevant always risk having missed a datum. Violation: a solver ignores an element, solves the gate anyway — this is luck, not understanding, and the next gate will kill them.

**Rule 4: A solution the solver cannot explain produces no learning.**
Lucky closure of a gate without understanding gives nothing. The gate may close. The next one at the same grade will kill. Violation: a solver exits a gate unable to describe what they did. The mystery person notes this. The reader sees what the solver cannot.

**Rule 5: The process transfers.**
Every mental operation useful inside a gate is useful outside it. This is not analogy. The operations are identical. The gate made the cost of not using them fatal — ordinary life merely makes the cost invisible. Violation: a solver applies a gate operation to ordinary life and it fails — this signals the operation was not genuinely understood, only performed.

**Rule 6: The wrong approach always feels right while it is happening.**
No wrong approach is stupid. Each one is a reasonable response to an incomplete understanding of the problem. Solvers who use wrong approaches are not failures — they are people who have not yet asked the right question. Violation: a wrong approach is portrayed as obviously wrong from the start. The reader must believe in the approach before it fails.

**Rule 7: The sitting down is the turning point.**
Every successful gate closure in this world involves a moment where the solver stops all action. This is not rest. It is the cessation of the wrong approach. The right question cannot arrive while the wrong approach is still running. The sitting down is the physical marker of this transition in prose. It may be literal or metaphorical but it must be present. Violation: a solver transitions directly from wrong approach to correct solution without stopping.

**Rule 8: Guilds are not wrong — they are limited.**
Guild methods work on the gate types that produced them. New gate structures invalidate old methods. The guild that cannot update its methods accumulates dangerous pedantry. Guilds are sympathetic institutions with real expertise that has become rigid. They are not villains. They are the institutionalised version of what every solver risks becoming. Violation: a guild is portrayed as stupid or corrupt rather than limited and rigid.

**Rule 9: The gate responds continuously, not only at closure.**
The gate is a biofeedback instrument. It shifts register throughout the solving attempt. Solvers who learn to read register shifts gain information about the quality of their current approach. Violation: a gate that gives no feedback until it closes or kills.

**Rule 10: Wrong approach type determines gate signature and mirror content, not solver identity.**
The same solver using two different wrong approaches produces two different gate signatures and two different mirror reflections. The same wrong approach used by two different solvers produces the same signature and the same mirror. The signature and mirror are in the approach, not the person. Violation: gate response varies based on who the solver is rather than what mental move they are making.

**Rule 11: The right question precedes the right action — and the gate confirms it.**
The gate enters Register 3 before the solver acts on the right question. The gate responds to the forming of the question, not the execution of the answer. A solver who acts correctly without having asked the right question will find the gate closing — and will learn nothing, because they cannot identify what changed. Violation: gate closes in response to correct physical action performed without the right question having formed.

**Rule 12: The mirror is always operating. It is never explained by the gate.**
The gate reorganises itself to reflect the solver's current mental frame at all times. It does not label what it is showing. It does not indicate that it is showing anything. A solver who cannot read the mirror experiences the gate as inert or arbitrary. A solver who can read it has been given the question by the gate itself. The mirror requires the same mental operations to read as the gate requires to close — a solver without working backwards cannot read a Grade 4 mirror. Violation: the gate indicates, labels, or draws attention to what it is reflecting. The mirror shows. It does not point.

## 5. THE GATE AS MIRROR
*The gate reads the solver's current mental process and reflects it back in the room's physical arrangement. This is not punishment and not reward. It is diagnosis made visible — but only to someone who can read it. The gate does not explain what it is showing. It shows. The solver who cannot read the mirror fails. The solver who reads it has been given the question by the gate itself.*

*This is the world's core externalisation mechanism. Interior mental process — invisible by nature — becomes readable through gate behaviour. The reader learns to read it before most characters do.*

---

### Gate Registers
*The gate operates in four observable registers. Register is not a label the characters use — it is a narrator description. Characters may develop their own words for it across arcs.*

| Register | What It Looks Like | What It Signals |
|---|---|---|
| Register 1 — Inert | The gate holds. Nothing responds to contact or movement. The room is exactly as it was. | Solver has not yet named the unknown. Wrong approach running confidently. |
| Register 2 — Receptive | Something shifts. Not dramatically. A texture changes. Light redistributes. Something that was background becomes foreground. The gate is not responding — it is waiting. | Wrong approaches exhausted. Solver has stopped. The sitting down has happened. |
| Register 3 — Convergent | Gate responds before the solver acts. The unknown reveals itself a half-second before the solver reaches for it. Parts that were separate acquire relationship. | Right question has formed, consciously or not. The gate confirms before the character knows. |
| Register 4 — Closure | Matter-of-fact. The gate closes the way a problem resolves when the right answer is given. No ceremony. | Solution enacted. |

*The reader learns to read registers. By Arc 3 they are diagnosing the solver's mental state from gate behaviour before any character names it.*

---

### The Mirror Mechanic
*The gate does not just register the solver's process — it reorganises itself to reflect that process back. The mirror is always operating. It is legible only to a solver who has stopped running their wrong approach and is looking at the room rather than acting on it.*

**How the mirror works:**

The gate's physical contents — objects, surfaces, light, spatial arrangement — shift to externalise the solver's current mental frame. The shift is not dramatic. It is precise. A solver deep in the executor approach will find the room arranged to mirror exactly what the executor approach assumes: obvious pattern emphasised, everything else receding. The room is telling them what they are doing. They cannot read it because they are still doing it.

When the solver sits down — when the wrong approach stops — the mirror shifts. Now it shows not what the solver was doing but what the room actually is. The structure beneath the surface pattern. The absence where they were looking for presence. The sitting down is the moment the mirror becomes readable, because it is the first moment the solver is looking at the room rather than acting on their assumption about it.

**The mirror is Socratic, not corrective.** It does not show the answer. It shows the shape of the wrong question. A solver who reads the mirror correctly sees: this is what I was assuming. Then the right question arrives — not from the mirror but from the gap between what the mirror showed and what the solver now sees.

**The mirror escalates across grades.** In Grade 1 gates the mirror is blunt — the room's arrangement almost directly shows the wrong assumption. In Grade 5+ gates the mirror is subtle and requires prior operations to read at all. A solver without working backwards cannot read a Grade 4 mirror. A solver without restatement cannot read a Grade 5 mirror. The mirror requires the same mental operations as the gate itself.

---

### Wrong Approach Mirror Behaviour
*What the gate reflects back during each wrong approach type. The mirror content is specific to the approach, not to the solver. Same approach, same mirror — across all solvers, all arcs, all gate grades.*

| Wrong Approach Type | Gate Signature | Mirror Content | What a Mirror-Reader Sees |
|---|---|---|---|
| The executor (acts immediately on obvious pattern) | Register 1 holds. Contact produces no response. | Room arranges itself around the obvious pattern the executor is following. That pattern is emphasised, clarified, made almost legible — and still the gate does not move. | The room is showing you what you assumed. The assumption is not wrong in itself. It is wrong as the frame. Something outside the pattern is not receding — it is waiting. |
| The system builder (applies method regardless of fit) | Register flickers between 1 and false 3. Parts light, nothing integrates. | Room mirrors the method's structure: every element processed, categorised, assigned. The categorisation is perfect. The gate does not care about the categorisation. | You have organised the room according to your method. The room is organised. The gate is elsewhere. |
| The information gatherer (catalogues everything, asks nothing) | Register 1, undisturbed. As if solver not yet present. | Room gives everything asked for. Complete. Still. The mirror is a perfect catalogue. | The room has answered every question you asked. You have not asked the question the room is waiting for. The mirror shows completeness. Completeness is not the same as the right question. |
| The confident specialist (applies domain expertise to wrong problem type) | Register 2 touched briefly, then lost. | Room mirrors the specialist's domain precisely — for as long as the domain applies. When it ends, the room stops mirroring. What remains is the part that was never in the domain. | Your expertise organised part of the room. The rest did not organise. The part that did not organise is the gate. |
| The hypothesis tester (tests systematically without naming what is being tested) | Register flickers rapidly. Each test briefly held, then dropped. | Room mirrors each hypothesis in sequence. Each is shown clearly. Each is also shown ending. The sequence of endings has a shape — but only visible if you stop and look at all of them together. | You have been shown ten mirrors in sequence. Each showed you what you assumed that time. Looked at together, they show what you have not yet assumed. |
| The force applier (pushes harder when path does not appear) | Register 1 throughout. Gate shows no fatigue. | Room does not mirror force. Force has no shape to reflect. The room is unchanged. | The gate is not tired. The gate is waiting for a question, not an action. Force is the absence of a question. The mirror has nothing to show. |

### Mirror Readability Progression
*The mirror is always operating. Who can read it changes across arcs.*

| Arc | Who Can Read the Mirror | What They See | What They Do With It |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | Nobody. Not even the successful solver. | The successful solver reads the mirror accidentally — the sitting down puts them in the right position, and the right question arrives without them knowing the mirror produced it. | Nothing. They exit. The mirror read them rather than the reverse. |
| 3-4 | Experienced solvers begin noticing that the room shifts. They cannot read the content yet — only that it shifts. | The shift itself becomes information: something changed when I stopped. | Some begin deliberately stopping earlier to see the shift. This is the beginning of mirror literacy. |
| 5-6 | A small number of solvers can read specific mirror content. Always the operations they already own. A solver with working backwards can read a Grade 4 mirror. A solver with restatement can read a Grade 5 mirror. | The wrong assumption externalised. The gap between assumption and reality made visible. | They use the mirror to find the right question faster. They do not understand why the mirror shows what it shows. They trust it. |
| Mystery person | Full mirror literacy. Reads mirror content across all approach types at all grades. | Not just what each mirror shows — but the pattern across thousands of mirrors. | Recording it. Building toward a restatement of the macro problem. The gates are not testing humanity. They are teaching it. The mystery person is the only one close to reading the lesson. |

### Gate Registers in Ordinary Life
*Ordinary life has no gate. The externalisation mechanism is different but parallel: other people.*

When a solver asks the right question in an ordinary context — a meeting, a conversation, a work problem — other people in the room respond before they know why. Someone goes still. A conversation changes register. The solver's question produces in other people the same shift the gate produces in Register 2: something that was background becomes foreground.

This is not magical. It is accurate. The right question reorganises the problem space — and other people in that space feel the reorganisation before they can name it.

*Agent instruction: Ordinary life echo scenes must show at least one other person's involuntary response to the right question. This response is never explained. It is described physically and briefly. The reader draws the connection to gate register behaviour.*

## 6. THE PRODUCTIVE FAILURE STRUCTURE
*This section governs chapter architecture. It is not optional. It is the mechanism by which the reader experiences the process rather than observing it.*

**The principle:** The reader must form wrong hypotheses naturally through the prose and exhaust them before the right question arrives. This is not a puzzle box. The reader is not hunting a hidden answer. They are experiencing the same failure modes as the characters — because the wrong approaches are described specifically enough to activate the reader's own problem-solving instincts.

**The chapter structure:**

1. Gate problem is described with enough specificity that the reader's instinct activates. The reader cannot help forming a hypothesis. This hypothesis is the same wrong instinct most people have — it feels obvious.

2. First solver enters and acts on the obvious hypothesis. It fails. The reader's hypothesis fails with them. The failure is specific and physical — not "it didn't work" but exactly what happened when it didn't work.

3. Second solver enters with a different wrong approach. This one also feels reasonable. The reader adjusts their hypothesis. This one also fails. Differently.

4. Third solver (optional) enters. The reader is running out of instincts. Something small is noticed in this failure that did not fit the previous frame — a detail, an absence, a texture that was wrong.

5. The right solver enters last. Or the third solver sits down. The reader has nothing left. The wrong approaches have been exhausted. The right question arrives into that emptiness. It lands as relief, not revelation.

6. Solution is quiet. Almost anticlimactic. Eleven seconds of work after forty minutes of wrong effort.

7. Ordinary life echo follows in the same chapter or the next. The same question applied to a real-world problem. Never explained. Reader makes the connection.

**What the productive failure structure must NOT do:**
- Break the fourth wall. Never address the reader directly. Never write "stop and think about this." The wrong hypotheses must be activated by the prose itself.
- Make the wrong approach obviously wrong from the start. The reader must believe in it before it fails.
- Explain why each approach failed. Show the physical consequence. Leave the diagnosis to the reader.
- Rush to the sitting down. The wrong approaches must run their full course before the solver stops.

## 7. TERMS BECOME LENSES

*Once a solver learns an operation through experience, they see it everywhere. This is how the reader generalises. The table below covers all operations in the curriculum. Writers must show each operation transferring to at least one non-gate context per arc in which it is active.*

| Operation | Inside Gates | Ordinary Workplace | Family / Domestic | Civic / Institutional |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Name the unknown | What the gate requires you to find before you touch anything. | What the meeting, project, or conflict actually needs — rarely what is stated. | What the argument is actually about underneath what is being said. | What the policy is actually trying to achieve versus what it states. |
| Find what is missing | The absence that the gate is built around. | The step, resource, or decision that was skipped and is causing everything downstream to fail. | The need that is not being expressed and is therefore not being met. | The stakeholder, data point, or condition that was left out of the plan. |
| Separate parts of condition | Identifying which clause of the gate's condition is blocking progress and which can be temporarily set aside. | Breaking a compound brief into its individual requirements and finding which one is actually blocking delivery. | Separating what someone is asking for from what they actually need from what they are able to receive right now. | Identifying which clause of a policy is the real constraint and which is ornamental. |
| Did you use all the data? | Noticing that one element of the gate interior has never been incorporated into any approach. | Noticing that one piece of available information was never brought into the decision. | Noticing that one person's perspective was never consulted. | Noticing that one existing precedent or resource was never considered. |
| Can you check the result? | Verifying the gate actually closed rather than assuming it did. | Testing the output against the original brief, not against the method used. | Checking whether the resolution actually addressed what the conflict was about. | Verifying that the policy achieves its stated objective, not just that it was implemented correctly. |
| Can you derive the result differently? | Asking whether a shorter path to gate closure exists after the gate is closed. | After finishing a project, asking whether a simpler or more elegant approach was available. | After resolving a conflict, asking what the faster path would have been. | After implementing a policy, asking whether a less expensive or more direct route would have produced the same result. |
| Look at the unknown | Standing at the gate entrance and naming the category of thing the gate wants before entering. | Naming what a project actually needs to produce before planning how to produce it. | Naming what an argument is actually trying to resolve before responding to its content. | Naming what a decision actually needs to determine before gathering information. |
| Related problem | A previous gate whose structure matches this one — used by method, result, or both. | A past project or negotiation with the same underlying shape. | A previous disagreement that resolved in a way that applies here. | A precedent, case study, or historical parallel with the same structural relationships. |
| Analogy — simpler analogous problem | Inventing a simpler gate with identical structural relations and solving it to find the method. | Inventing a simpler version of a project with identical dependencies to find the workflow. | Simplifying a family conflict to its essential structure — different people, same dynamic — to find what resolved it before. | Finding a simpler precedent with identical structural constraints and reading how it was resolved. |
| Looking back / transfer | Staying after the gate closes to ask what the method was and where else it applies. | Debriefing after delivery: what actually worked, and what problem structure does it apply to? | After resolution: what did we actually do, and what does that tell us about this kind of conflict? | After implementation: what did we learn about this type of problem that we can carry into future policy? |
| Decompose condition | Holding only one part of the gate's compound condition and finding what is determined by that part alone. | Isolating one requirement of a complex brief and asking what is achievable if only that requirement is in scope. | Taking one issue from a compound conflict and resolving it independently before returning to the whole. | Taking one clause of a compound policy objective and testing what is achievable under that clause alone. |
| Introduce auxiliary elements | Adding something not given in the gate — a construction, a sub-unknown — to create a bridge to a known method. | Adding a intermediate deliverable not in the original brief because it makes the path to the final deliverable visible. | Introducing a mediating question or a third party not originally part of the conflict because it creates a path to resolution. | Introducing a pilot, a working group, or a precedent that is not the policy itself but makes the policy achievable. |
| Can you use the result? | After closing a gate, asking what new gates the method opens. | After delivering a project, asking what related problems the approach now makes accessible. | After resolving one conflict, asking what related conflicts the resolution method now makes approachable. | After implementing a policy, asking what related problems the same mechanism could address. |
| Auxiliary problem (invented) | Deliberately constructing a different gate problem as a stepping stone, knowing it may not succeed. | Deliberately creating a sub-project that is not the deliverable but creates conditions for the deliverable to become possible. | Deliberately introducing a side conversation that is not the conflict but creates conditions for the conflict to become resolvable. | Deliberately introducing a pilot or amendment that is not the policy but creates conditions for the policy to pass. |
| Specialisation | Contracting the gate problem to its simplest specific case to find a foothold. | Contracting a general process problem to its simplest specific instance to find what actually works. | Taking the most specific, least charged version of a conflict to find what resolution looks like. | Testing a policy at its smallest scale to find what implementation actually requires. |
| Working backwards | Assuming the gate is closed and asking what must have been true immediately before closure. | Starting from the required outcome and asking what must be true one step before it. | Imagining the resolved version of a conflict and asking what changed from now to then. | Starting from the desired policy outcome and working back to what must be in place for it to be achievable. |
| Generalisation | Expanding the gate problem to find the structure it belongs to, then using that structure's known solution. | Expanding a specific project problem to find the general class of problem it belongs to, then applying known solutions for that class. | Recognising a specific conflict as an instance of a general type and applying what is known about that type. | Recognising a specific policy challenge as an instance of a general governance problem with known solutions. |
| Inventor's Paradox | Making the gate problem more ambitious to find the structure that makes the specific version impossible to solve. | Making the project scope bigger to find the architecture that makes the specific deliverable tractable. | Making the conflict explicit and general rather than specific and personal to find the resolution structure. | Making the policy objective more ambitious to find the systemic change that makes the specific objective achievable. |
| Reductio ad absurdum | Assuming the gate cannot be closed and deriving a contradiction — which proves it can be. | Assuming a problem has no solution and following the consequences until a constraint is violated — then working from that violation. | Assuming a conflict cannot be resolved and following the consequences until something becomes unacceptable — then working from that point. | Assuming a policy is impossible and following the consequences until a contradiction with existing law, precedent, or resources emerges. |
| Variation / restatement | Recognising the gate as stated is not the real gate. Restating the condition until it matches what the gate actually requires. | Recognising the project brief as stated is not the real brief. Finding the actual requirement beneath the stated one. | Recognising the stated complaint is not the real complaint. Finding what is actually wanted beneath what is said. | Recognising the stated policy objective is not the real objective. Finding the actual constraint or need beneath the stated goal. |
| Heuristic vs proof | Following a provisional path through the gate knowing it may not be rigorous — and separately verifying that it is correct. | Acting on a plausible plan before all evidence is in, while tracking separately what needs to be verified once the plan runs. | Committing to a provisional resolution before full understanding — while tracking what remains to be checked. | Acting on a plausible policy before full evidence — while building in verification mechanisms. |
| Subconscious work | Leaving the gate and returning — the right question is present on return. | Leaving a problem, doing something unrelated, returning to find the path visible. | Stepping away from a conflict, letting it sit, returning to find the tone of resolution possible. | Suspending work on a policy problem, letting it rest, returning to find a new framing available. |
| Auxiliary problem chains | Constructing a sequence of equivalent reductions through the gate, each solvable, synthesised back to the original. | Constructing a sequence of equivalent sub-problems, each more tractable, synthesised back to deliver the original. | Constructing a sequence of smaller resolutions, each building on the last, until the original conflict is addressable. | Constructing a sequence of smaller policy steps, each achievable, that collectively achieve the original objective. |
