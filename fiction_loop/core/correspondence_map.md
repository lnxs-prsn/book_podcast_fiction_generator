# DOCUMENT 0: CORRESPONDENCE MAP
*Build this first. Every story element must trace to a real mechanic in Pólya's process with the same failure mode. If it doesn't, it is decoration, not architecture.*

*Agent instruction: Before generating any chapter, verify the gate problem and the wrong approaches in the chapter brief map to entries in Section 3. If they do not, return to Orchestrator.*

## STEP 1 — THE TWO SYSTEMS

| Field | Answer |
|---|---|
| Story world / genre | Portal gate fiction — contemporary world, no superpowers, gates open randomly into enclosed problem-spaces. Death on failure. Normal society continues outside. |
| Subject domain | Pólya's heuristic — the mental operations useful in solving problems, as described in How to Solve It. |
| Core equation | The gate IS a problem. Not a monster encounter. Not a dungeon. A structured problem with unknowns, data, and conditions. Pólya's process IS the only reliable method for closing it. |
| What the process IS in this system | The invisible physics of the world. It operates whether or not anyone has named it. Characters who use it survive. Characters who do not, die or fail. |
| What the world's governing force IS | Gates — unbounded, varied, escalating in structural complexity. Why they exist is the macro mystery. |

*The core equation uses IS, not LIKE. The moment you say LIKE, the mechanics become optional. They must be load-bearing.*

## SECTION 1 — CORE INFRASTRUCTURE

| Story Element | Domain Concept | Shared Mechanical Properties | Shared Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| The gate | A problem (to find or prove) | Has unknowns, data, conditions. Structured. Solvable. Resists any approach that does not address the actual unknown. | Ignored conditions = gate does not close. Misread unknown = wrong solution, death. |
| Gate interior | The problem space | Contains all necessary data. Nothing inside is decorative — everything is either data, condition, or distractor. | Treating distractor as data wastes effort and time. Missing real data = incomplete solution. |
| Gate difficulty grade | Problem complexity | Grade 1: clear unknown, obvious data. Grade 5+: ambiguous unknowns, contradictory conditions, requires working backwards or restatement. | Applying Grade 1 methods to Grade 4 gates kills. Attempting Grade 5 with Grade 2 vocabulary produces confident wrong answers. |
| The wrong approach | Pólya's bad solver patterns | Each wrong approach is a specific variety of failed problem-solving: acting before understanding, solving the wrong problem, gathering data without naming the unknown, testing hypotheses without knowing what is being tested. | Gate does not respond. Time runs out. Death. |
| The sitting down | The deliberate pause before the right question | Physical marker of the transition from wrong approach to right question. Appears in every successful solver. | When this pause does not happen the wrong approach continues until time runs out. |
| Guilds / response infrastructure | Institutionalised heuristic — and its corruption into pedantry | Guild methods codify what worked on previous gate types. Work on familiar grades. Fail on new structures. | Pedantry: applying a rule rigidly in cases where it does not fit. Confident failure. |
| Normal society outside | The domain where the process transfers | Same mental operations apply to work, family, civic problems. No superpowers needed. Process is universal. | Character solves gates but not life = process learned as trick, not principle. Transfer never happened. |
| The mystery person | The accumulated pattern across many iterations | Has observed more gate events than any single solver. Sees the process operating across varied surfaces. Identity is itself a problem for the reader to solve. | Cannot be understood from a single observation. Meaning only emerges through accumulation. |

## SECTION 2 — PROGRESSION STATES

*Gate grade IS the progression system. Each grade requires a mental operation the previous grade did not demand. The failure mode of grade N is what makes grade N+1 necessary.*

| Gate Grade | Problem Structure | Mental Operations Required | Core Truth | Failure Mode | Why Previous Grade's Approach Fails Here |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | One unknown, data obvious, condition explicit. | Name the unknown before acting. Separate parts of condition. Find what is missing rather than cataloguing what is present. | Most problems fail at the first step: acting before understanding what is actually wanted. | Rushing to action. Matching what is visible without asking what is absent. | Entry point. |
| 2 | Unknown clear but connection to data non-obvious. One datum consistently unused. | Look at the unknown. Do you know a related problem? Use its method or result. Did you use all the data? | You are never solving alone. Every problem has ancestors. The unused datum is the gate. | No prior knowledge mobilised. Stuck with only direct approach. One element of data never incorporated. | Grade 1 direct action finds no path because the connection between data and unknown is not visible without prior knowledge. |
| 3 | Condition has multiple parts. Full condition unsolvable directly. Simpler analogous structure exists. | Decompose: keep part of condition, drop others. Analogy: construct a simpler problem with identical structural relations. Introduce auxiliary elements. Ask: can you derive the result differently? | A problem you cannot solve whole you may solve in parts. A simpler problem with the same structure shows the method. | Treating the full condition as a single unit. Never asking whether a simpler version exists with the same relations. | Recalled related problem does not match the full condition. No bridge between decomposed parts found. |
| 4 | No directly accessible foothold. Forward path exhausted within reach of known methods. Domain ends before gate does. | Invent an auxiliary problem as a deliberate stepping stone. Specialise to find the simplest case. Ask: can you use the result? | The direct path is not always the right path. A detour invented for purpose is not weakness — it is method. | Pushing harder on exhausted forward path. Stopping at domain boundary. Never inventing a detour — only trying to recall one. | Decomposition produces parts with no connecting structure. Analogous problem does not exist in memory and is never constructed. |
| 5 | Unknown not reachable from data side. Forward reasoning dead-ends. The specific problem may be harder than its generalisation. | Working backwards: assume solution exists, chain back to known ground. Generalise to find the structure. Inventor's Paradox: the more ambitious problem may be easier. Reductio ad absurdum: assume the opposite, derive the contradiction. | Start from the end. The more ambitious problem may be more accessible. Assuming the opposite can prove what direct reasoning cannot. | Forward reasoning only. Refusing to assume what is not yet proven. Restricting to the specific case when the general structure would have opened it. | Invented stepping stone reaches a point with no forward connection to the unknown. |
| 6 | Problem as stated is wrong. Valid solution produces no gate closure. | Variation of the problem: restate until the condition matches what the gate actually requires. | The most dangerous gate looks solvable but isn't — as stated. | Solving the stated problem correctly and stopping. Testing variations of the wrong statement indefinitely. | Backwards chain from wrong statement produces confident wrong answer. Gate holds at Register 3 without closing. |
| 7 | Method found but cannot be accounted for. Or: method works but solver cannot distinguish where it applies and where it does not. | Heuristic vs proof distinction: following the provisional path is not the same as verifying it. Subconscious work: the right question arrives after stopping, not during forcing. | Finding the path is not verifying the path. Stopping all effort is sometimes the necessary step. | Treating heuristic confidence as proof. Or: refusing to act without proof first, paralysed by heuristic evidence. Forcing when wrong approaches are exhausted. | Correct restatement produces a problem the solver can approach heuristically but cannot verify or apply to the next gate. |
| 8 | Multiple equivalent reductions required. No single step sufficient. Solution requires constructing and reversing a chain. | Auxiliary problem chains: analysis builds the chain forward to something solvable; synthesis walks back through the chain. Both are required for full closure. | The solution is constructed step by step and verified by retracing. Analysis invents; synthesis delivers. | Treating the chain endpoint as the answer. Building the chain forward without knowing how to reverse it. | Single auxiliary problem solves a sub-problem but leaves the original gate unclosed. |

## SECTION 3 — WRONG APPROACH TYPES
*This section is the core of process fiction. Every chapter must show at least two wrong approaches before the right question arrives. Each wrong approach maps to a specific Pólya-identified failure pattern.*

*Agent instruction: When assembling a chapter prompt, select wrong approaches from this table that have NOT yet been shown in the current context type. Track used approaches in process_state.json. Each wrong approach must feel competent and reasonable before it fails — never obviously wrong from the start.*

| Wrong Approach Name | What the Solver Does | Pólya Failure Pattern | Physical Appearance in Prose | Gate Mirror Content | Why It Feels Right While Doing It |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| The executor | Acts immediately on the most obvious pattern without naming the unknown. | Embarking on action before understanding the problem. | Fast movement. Confident handling of objects. No pause at the entrance. | Room emphasises the obvious pattern — makes it cleaner, more legible. One element outside the pattern is not receding. | Looks like competence. Decisiveness. Experience. |
| The system builder | Has a method and applies it regardless of whether it fits this problem. | Applying a rule rigidly in cases where it does not fit. Pedantry. | Methodical, thorough, unhurried. Creates order from chaos visibly. | Room mirrors the method's structure: everything categorised. Categorisation is perfect. Gate is elsewhere. | Works on familiar problem types. Feels like the right professional approach. |
| The information gatherer | Collects all available data meticulously before acting. Never asks what the data is for. | Gathering data without naming the unknown. Confusing information for understanding. | Clipboard. Notes. Systematic audit. Complete documentation. | Room gives complete data. Still. The catalogue is the mirror. | Feels responsible. Thorough. Like proper preparation before action. |
| The hypothesis tester | Generates and tests alternatives systematically. Never stops to ask what is actually being tested. | Testing solutions without understanding the problem. Varying the method without varying the question. | Tries one thing, observes, tries another. Rapid iteration. Scientific-looking. | Room mirrors each hypothesis in sequence. Sequence of endings has a shape — visible only if you stop and look at all of them together. | Feels like good problem-solving. Adaptive. Empirical. |
| The confident specialist | Applies deep expertise from one domain to a problem that is not in that domain. | Misidentifying the problem type. Expertise blindness. | Moves with authority. References past successes. Dismisses elements that do not fit their frame. | Room organises by domain boundary. Domain elements grouped. Gap between groups contains the unknown. Solver looks inside the groups. | Has worked before. Expertise is real. Just not applicable here. |
| The force applier | Attempts to overpower the problem rather than understand it. | Substituting effort or persistence for understanding. | Physical exertion. Trying harder when the same approach fails repeatedly. | Force has no shape. Room does not mirror force. Mirror shows the same room unchanged. | Persistence is usually rewarded. Feels like not giving up. |
| The executor on complex condition | Acts immediately on the first part of a multi-part condition, ignores the others. | Treating a compound condition as if only its most obvious clause matters. | Solves visibly, efficiently, completely — one part. Then stops. The gate does not close. | Room mirrors the first part only — makes it prominent, complete. Other parts dim but present. | Solved part of it. That should count for something. Surely the rest follows. |
| The system builder on complex condition | Processes all parts of a multi-part condition equally and simultaneously. Finds no path because the method cannot prioritise. | Refusing to decompose: treating every clause as equally essential. | Methodical. Touches every element. Completes a full pass. Nothing integrates. | Room mirrors all parts equally — even, flat, no gradient. The part that matters most is indistinguishable from the rest. | Thoroughness feels like correctness. Every part deserves equal attention. |
| The perfectionist | Refuses to commit to any approach without certainty first. Waits for proof before reasoning provisionally. | Refusing heuristic reasoning. Requiring verification before exploration. | Stillness. Analysis paralysis. Visible deliberation with no action. | Room unchanged. Waiting for certainty produces no mirror content — the gate shows nothing because nothing was offered. | Feels rigorous. Responsible. Not wanting to waste effort on a wrong path. |
| The variation-tester | Tests restatements of the problem systematically. All fail because the wrong problem is being restated — the restatements are variations of an incorrect statement. | Restating the wrong problem rather than questioning whether the problem itself is wrong. | Tries different angles on the same gate. Changes approach repeatedly. Nothing holds. | Room mirrors each variation showing the same remainder unsolved. | Feels like creative problem-solving. Adaptive. Refusing to give up. |
| The single-step auxiliary solver | Invents a stepping stone problem and solves it. Treats the stepping stone as the destination. The original gate does not close. | Mistaking the auxiliary for the solution. Failing to synthesise back to the original. | Solves the auxiliary with visible satisfaction. Returns to original gate. Confused. | Room mirrors the auxiliary solved — and shows the original problem unchanged beyond it. | The stepping stone worked. That should have been enough. |
| The planner without synthesis | Constructs a chain of auxiliary problems correctly in analysis. Cannot reverse the chain to synthesise back to the original. | Analysis without synthesis. Building the forward chain without the return path. | Constructs the chain visibly, methodically. Arrives at the solvable endpoint. Cannot proceed. | Room mirrors the chain forward — and the direction of return, which the planner does not look at. | The chain is correct. The analysis was sound. The solution should be there. |
| The heuristic-only solver | Finds a method that works. Cannot account for it. Uses it on the next gate of the same grade where it does not apply. Fails because they cannot distinguish where the method applies and where it does not. | Mistaking heuristic confidence for proof. No verification. No understanding of scope. | Closes gates efficiently. Cannot explain what they did. On the gate where conditions differ: identical approach, gate does not close. | Room mirrors the method working — and shows its limits. Solver reads only the working. | It worked before. It should work again. The approach was the same. |
| The guild verifier | Has access to a method that would work but demands formal proof before acting on it. Refuses to proceed on heuristic evidence. Paralysed. | Refusing heuristic reasoning in contexts where only provisional reasoning is available. | Visible deliberation. Defers to process. The time runs out while the proof is being constructed. | Room unchanged. Refusal to commit produces no engagement. | Feels principled. Rigorous. Not wanting to establish a dangerous precedent by acting without verification. |

## SECTION 4 — THE RIGHT QUESTION
*The right question is not a solution. It is the question that makes the solution visible. It always arrives after the wrong approaches have been exhausted. In prose it arrives as a relief, not a revelation. One sentence. The solution follows in seconds or minutes.*

| The Right Question | What It Replaces | Physical Marker in Prose | Pólya Operation |
|---|---|---|---|
| What is the unknown here, really? | What should I do with what I see? | The sitting down. Hands still. Looking at structure rather than contents. | Identify the unknown. |
| What is actually missing? | What is here? | Looking at gaps rather than contents. Gaze moving to where things are not. | Find what is absent over what is present. |
| Which part of this condition matters most? What can I hold if I drop the rest? | How do I satisfy the whole condition? | Deliberately looking away from most of the room. Narrowing attention visibly. | Separate parts of condition / decompose. |
| Have I used everything that was given? | I have a solution. That should be enough. | Returning to the room's edge after a partial solution. Touching what was not touched. | Did you use all the data? |
| Does this answer actually satisfy the original condition? | I completed the method. | Standing at the gate after the solution and waiting. Not assuming closure. | Can you check the result? |
| Is there a shorter path? A cleaner one? | The path I found is the path. | Stillness after closure. Not leaving. Looking back at what was done. | Can you derive the result differently? |
| What does this look like if I focus only on what is wanted — not where it comes from? | I need to start from what I have. | Standing at the entrance. Not moving toward the data. Eyes on the unknown's location. | Look at the unknown. |
| What does this remind me of? What have I done when the unknown looked like this? | I have never seen anything like this. | The unfocused gaze of someone searching memory, not the room. | Do you know a related problem? |
| I have seen something like this before. Can I use its result? Its method? Both? | I remember it but I cannot connect it to this. | Taking something from memory and holding it next to the current problem. Comparing structure. | Here is a problem related to yours and solved before. |
| What simpler problem has exactly the same structure as this one? | I have to solve this specific problem directly. | The solver mentally constructs a smaller version. Eyes unfocus — looking at structure, not content. | Analogy — simpler analogous problem. |
| What did I actually do? Where else does that apply? | The gate is closed. I am done. | Remaining after others leave. Hands still. Sometimes writing. Not confusion — inventory. | Looking back / transfer. |
| What if I set aside everything I cannot solve and hold only what I can? | I need to solve all of it before I can solve any of it. | Deliberately turning away from most of the room. One element examined while the rest is ignored. | Decompose condition / keep part drop part. |
| What needs to be added that is not here? What bridge does not exist yet? | I can only work with what was given. | Hands moving to the edge of the room — not to existing elements but to the space between them. | Introduce auxiliary elements. |
| What new problem does this solution open? What else becomes possible? | The gate is closed. That is the end. | Pausing at the gate exit. Looking back in. Not done yet. | Can you use the result? |
| What if I stopped trying to solve this and invented a different problem that gets me there? | This path is not working. I must push harder on this path. | The solver stops, physically steps back from what they were doing. Starts something that looks unrelated. | Auxiliary problem — invented stepping stone. |
| What if I tried the simplest possible version of this? | I need to solve the full thing. Starting from a simpler version feels like retreat. | Solver physically reduces the scope of what they are examining. Ignores most of the room to look at a small part. | Specialisation. |
| What would have to be true if this were already solved? | What can I do from where I am? | Physically turning away from the problem before reasoning. Standing with back to the data. | Working backwards / analysis. |
| What if this problem is easier if I make it bigger? | This specific problem is what I have to solve. | Solver steps back from the gate interior. Looks at it from a distance. Asks a question about all gates rather than this one. | Generalisation / Inventor's Paradox. |
| What if I assume the opposite of what I want and follow it until something breaks? | I cannot prove anything until I prove it directly. | Solver begins building a case for the thing they know is wrong. Follows it precisely. Waits for the contradiction. | Reductio ad absurdum. |
| Is this the real problem? Is what I have been trying to solve what the gate actually wants? | How do I solve this problem? | Long stillness. The frown that is not frustration but suspicion. | Variation of the problem / restatement. |
| This is a path. I can follow it now and verify it separately. Both are needed. | I cannot act until I have proof. Or: I have a method, therefore I have a proof. | Solver begins moving with visible deliberateness — not certainty. Keeps notes. | Heuristic reasoning vs proof distinction. |
| I need to stop. The right question is not arriving while I push. | Stopping feels like giving up. | Solver puts everything down. Leaves the gate interior entirely or sits at the wall. Returns later. The question is present on return. | Subconscious work. |
| This is not one step. It is a chain. And I need to be able to walk back through it. | I found the endpoint. That should be enough. | Solver constructs a sequence visibly. Then pauses. Turns around and traces the sequence backward before acting. | Auxiliary problem chains / Pappus synthesis. |

## SECTION 5 — THE ORDINARY LIFE ECHO
*Every gate event must have an ordinary life echo in the same chapter or the following chapter. The echo is not explained. The reader makes the connection themselves.*

*The echo is not an application of a lesson. It is the same problem wearing different clothes. The solver does not know they are using the gate method. They are simply thinking. At least one other person in the echo scene goes still, or the conversation changes register, when the right question forms — without explanation.*

| Gate Operation | Ordinary Life Echo Domain | How the Same Structure Appears | What the Reader Sees Without Being Told |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identify unknown / data / condition | Workplace | A problem at work approached with action when the real unknown was never named. | The solver pauses before opening the first file. Asks what they are actually trying to find. Someone in the room goes still. |
| What is missing (absence over presence) | Family / domestic | A conflict addressed by listing grievances when the real question is what is absent. | The solver stops arguing about what happened and asks what is actually needed. The other person goes quiet. |
| Separate the parts of the condition | Workplace / project | A project stalled because the whole brief is being treated as a single unit. | The solver writes down each requirement separately. Crosses out all but one. Solves that one. Returns to the rest. |
| Did you use all the data? | Professional | A decision made without using one available piece of information. The decision fails for exactly that reason. | The solver returns to materials already reviewed. Finds the thing that was never incorporated. |
| Can you check the result? | Any domain | An action taken that was assumed to have succeeded because the method was correct. | The solver does not assume. Checks. Finds the place where correct method produced incorrect result. |
| Can you derive the result differently? | Creative / professional | A solution found by a long route. The solver stays with it, looking for the simpler version. | The solver rewrites. The second version is shorter. The solver cannot explain why they looked for it. |
| Look at the unknown | Civic / institutional | A plan built from available resources when the starting point should have been what is actually required. | The solver writes one sentence: what is the outcome we need? Everything else follows from that. |
| Do you know a related problem? | Professional | A new challenge that looks unprecedented. The solver recognises the structure. | The solver names something from a different context. Uses that context's method. It works. Nobody else saw the resemblance. |
| Here is a problem related to yours and solved before | Professional / mentoring | The resemblance is already on the table — a precedent, an old file, a colleague's finished case. The work is not recalling it but adapting it: its result, its method, or the one missing piece that makes it usable. | The solver lays the old solution beside the new problem and maps it point by point. Takes the method, not the answer. Adds one thing the old case never needed. It fits. |
| Analogy — simpler analogous problem | Teaching / parenting | A complex explanation failing. The solver constructs a simpler version with identical relations. | The solver stops mid-sentence. Starts again with a different example. The listener understands immediately. |
| Looking back / transfer | After any completion | A project or conflict resolved. The solver stays with it afterward, asking what was actually done. | The solver does not move on immediately. Writes something down. Nobody asks what. |
| Decompose condition / keep part drop part | Negotiation / project | A negotiation stalled because all conditions are being treated as equally essential. | The solver asks: which one of these actually matters most right now? The conversation changes register. |
| Introduce auxiliary elements | Family / domestic | A conflict with no apparent path to resolution. The solver introduces a question or resource that was not part of the original conflict. | The solver says something that seems off-topic. The conversation moves toward it. Resolution follows. |
| Can you use the result? | Professional | A problem solved. The solver notices the method opens three other problems they have been avoiding. | The solver does not close the file. Opens three new ones. |
| Auxiliary problem | Project management | A project with no direct path to delivery. The solver proposes a sub-project that is not the deliverable but creates conditions for delivery. | The solver suggests something that seems like a detour. The team is unconvinced. Weeks later: the detour was the path. |
| Specialisation | Teaching / mentoring | A general principle not landing. The solver contracts it to its simplest case. | The solver gives one example. The simplest possible one. The principle becomes visible. |
| Working backwards / analysis | Civic / institutional | A plan built from available resources when the starting point should have been the required outcome. | The solver starts from where they need to end up and reasons back to first steps. The plan looks completely different. |
| Generalisation | Professional | A specific problem resisting solution. The solver recognises it as a case of a general class. The class has a known handling. The specific case follows. | The solver reframes the problem at a higher level. The team thinks they have lost ground. They have gained it. |
| Inventor's Paradox | Civic / professional | A goal repeatedly narrowed to make it "manageable," failing each time it shrinks. The solver expands it instead — the more ambitious version has structure the narrow one hid. | The solver proposes what sounds like more work. The room resists. The larger framing is the one that finally moves — and lands earlier than the narrow one ever would have. |
| Reductio ad absurdum | Argument / debate | A claim the solver wants to disprove. The solver argues for it instead. Follows it until it breaks. | The solver agrees with the person they are disagreeing with. Follows the agreement precisely. Shows where it leads. |
| Variation of the problem / restatement | Professional / civic | A long-running project failing. The solver asks whether the stated objective is the real objective. | The solver writes the objective at the top of a page. Below it writes: is this actually what we need? Silence in the room. |
| Heuristic reasoning vs proof distinction | Professional | A decision made on good evidence but without certainty. The solver acts while tracking what remains unverified. | The solver commits and moves. Also keeps a separate note: what we still need to check. |
| Subconscious work | Any domain | A problem the solver has been pushing on. They stop. Do something unrelated. Return. The path is visible. | The solver closes the file. Days later reopens it. Solves it in the first hour. Cannot explain why it was hard before. |
| Auxiliary problem chains | Complex project | A complex project requiring a sequence of enabling moves. The solver constructs and reverses the chain. | The solver maps the sequence on paper. Then reads it backward. Delivery begins at the end of the map, not the beginning. |

## SECTION 6 — TIME SCALING

| Domain Operation | Story-Time Equivalent | Emotional Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Recognising the unknown immediately | A breath. A glance around the gate interior before moving. | Reflex — the solver who sees this fast has done it many times. |
| The wrong approach running its course | Minutes to half an hour. The main body of the failure sequence. | Active effort. Feels productive. Is not. |
| The sitting down | A moment. Forced. | The hardest thing. Stopping when stopping feels like giving up. |
| The right question arriving | A moment. Sudden. After the sitting down. | Only lands with weight if the wrong approaches were fully shown. Cheap without them. |
| Carrying out the solution | Minutes. Quiet. | Less dramatic than finding the question. Almost anticlimactic. |
| Looking back after gate closes | Minutes to an hour. Private. Often skipped. | The quiet habit that separates compounding solvers from survivors. |
| Transfer to ordinary life | Days to weeks after gate event. | The proof. The process is not about gates. |
| Full internalisation | Years. Possibly a lifetime. | The mystery person is here. They no longer perform the process. They are it. |

## SECTION 7 — PROSE TRANSLATION EXAMPLES

### Example 1 — The Wrong Approach (The Executor)

| Standard Portal Genre Version | Process Fiction Version |
|---|---|
| Odion stepped through the gate. The room held forty numbered boxes and a panel with forty numbered slots. He understood immediately. One to one. He began placing boxes in slots, working fast and systematic, the way fifteen years of clearing had trained him to work. By box twelve the panel glowed red. He stopped. Something was wrong with his approach. He tried a different sequence. | Odion stepped through the gate. The room held forty numbered boxes and a panel with forty numbered slots. He understood immediately — or thought he did. Box one into slot one. Box two into slot two. He worked fast, the way fifteen years had trained him to work. Not asking. Moving. The boxes were numbered. The slots were numbered. The connection seemed obvious. It had seemed obvious for twenty minutes when the panel went red. He stopped. He looked at what he had done. He had been solving the problem he recognised instead of the problem that was there. He did not know yet that these were different problems. He only knew the panel was red. |

**What carries over:** The executor's confidence IS the failure. Speed and decisiveness are not wrong in themselves — they are wrong here because they substitute for the question that was never asked.

### Example 2 — The Sitting Down

| Standard Portal Genre Version | Process Fiction Version |
|---|---|
| She had tried everything. Nothing worked. She sat on the floor, frustrated, and tried to think of a new approach. Then she noticed the gap on shelf seven. | She sat on the floor. Not because she had decided to think. Because she had run out of things to try and her legs were tired of moving. She looked at her hands. She had been using them continuously for forty minutes and they had accomplished nothing. She made herself stop looking at what was in the room. She looked at what the room wanted. Not the boxes. Not the slots. What did the room want that it did not have? The question arrived without ceremony. The answer arrived eleven seconds after that. |

**What carries over:** The sitting down is not a technique. It is the cessation of the wrong approach. The right question cannot arrive while the wrong approach is still running.

### Example 3 — The Ordinary Life Echo

| Showing the Transfer (Wrong — explicit) | Showing the Transfer (Right — invisible) |
|---|---|
| The next morning at work, Kofi applied what he had learned in the gate. He asked what was missing instead of cataloguing what was present, just as he had done inside. | The next morning Kofi's manager put a logistics problem on his desk. Three warehouses, one missing shipment, two weeks of routing built around inventory that was not there. Kofi sat down. He put his hands flat on the desk. He did not open the first file. He looked at the problem the way he had learned — without yet knowing he had learned — to look at problems. What is not here that should be? He found it in four minutes. |
