## 1. VOICE — THREE ABSOLUTE RULES

### Rule 1: Declarative and short
Short sentences. Active voice. No hedging. The narrator observes — does not explain, does not interpret, does not reach for meaning before the reader is ready.

**WRONG:**
*"Although she had never encountered a gate of this particular configuration before, she found herself instinctively reaching for the understanding that the problem might require a different approach."*

**RIGHT:**
*"She had never seen a gate laid out like this. She stopped. She looked at it the way she looked at everything new — from the entrance, before moving."*

### Rule 2: Physical before philosophical
Every abstract operation is grounded in what the character does with their body, their eyes, their hands, their stillness. The concept lives in action before it lives in thought.

| Abstract Concept | Physical Anchor in This Story | Gate Register |
|---|---|---|
| Not knowing the unknown | Moving immediately. Touching things before looking at the whole room. Fast hands. | Register 1 — inert. Gate holds. |
| Naming the unknown | Standing still at the entrance. Looking at the room before entering it fully. Sometimes saying it aloud, to no one. | Transition toward Register 2. |
| The wrong approach running | Continuous movement. Effort. The look of someone who is working. | Register 1 throughout. Or false Register 3 flicker for system builder. |
| The sitting down | Literal or near-literal. Hands still. Eyes unfocused or looking at structure rather than contents. | Register 2 — gate shifts. Something that was background becomes foreground. |
| The right question arriving | A pause after the sitting down. Brief. Then movement that is different — directed, quiet, fast. | Register 3 — gate responds before solver acts. The unknown reveals itself a half-second early. |
| Looking back | Remaining after others leave. Stillness that looks like confusion but is not. Sometimes writing. | Gate already in Register 4. Solver reading back through what changed. |
| Transfer to ordinary life | The pause before opening the first file. Hands flat on the desk. Looking at the whole problem before touching any part of it. | No gate. Substitute signal: at least one other person in the room goes still, or the conversation changes register, before the solver has spoken the right question aloud. |
| Searching memory for related problem | Unfocused gaze. Not at the room — through it. Brief stillness mid-action. | Gate holds at Register 1 but with a different quality — receptive stillness rather than inert stillness. Subtle. Only visible in contrast to executor's Register 1. |
| Decomposing a condition | Gaze narrows. Solver begins ignoring parts of the room deliberately. Visible reduction of attention field. | Gate dims in areas the solver has set aside. The area of attention brightens fractionally. |

### Rule 3: Negative space
The gap between observation and meaning is the teaching moment. Do not close it. The reader who connects the gate experience to the ordinary life moment themselves has learned more than the reader who was told.

**WRONG — closing the gap:**
*"She realised that what she had done in the gate — asking what was missing rather than cataloguing what was present — was exactly what she needed to do here."*

**RIGHT — leaving the gap:**
*"She did not open the first file. She looked at the problem the way she had looked at the room. What is not here that should be? She found it in four minutes."*

## 2. THE PRODUCTIVE FAILURE ARCHITECTURE
*This is the structural rule unique to process fiction. It does not exist in thing fiction. It governs how every gate scene is built.*

### The core principle
The reader must form wrong hypotheses naturally through the prose and exhaust them before the right question arrives. The wrong approaches are described specifically enough to activate the reader's own problem-solving instincts. The reader fails alongside the characters — not because they are told to think, but because the prose puts them in the same position as the solver.

### The fourth wall is absolute
Never address the reader directly. Never write "stop and think." Never write "what would you try?" Never write "you might wonder." The reader's hypotheses must be activated by the specificity of the gate description and the believability of the wrong approaches — never by instruction.

**WRONG:**
*"Stop reading for a moment. You are the next person through the gate. What do you try?"*

**RIGHT:**
*[Describe the gate problem with enough specificity that the reader cannot help forming a hypothesis. Then show a character acting on that hypothesis and failing. The reader's hypothesis fails with the character.]*

### The wrong approach must feel right
Every wrong approach must be portrayed as reasonable, competent, and natural. The reader must believe in it before it fails. If the wrong approach looks obviously wrong from the start, it teaches nothing — the reader has not formed the hypothesis, so the failure does not land.

**WRONG:**
*"Odion charged in without thinking, randomly grabbing boxes."*

**RIGHT:**
*"Odion saw it immediately. Forty boxes. Forty slots. Numbers on both. He began placing them in order, fast and systematic, the way fifteen years of clearing had trained him to work."*

### The failure must be specific
When the wrong approach fails, show exactly what happened. Not "it didn't work." The physical, specific consequence of this particular wrong approach meeting this particular gate structure.

**WRONG:**
*"The gate didn't respond to his attempts."*

**RIGHT:**
*"At box twelve the panel made a sound he had not heard before. A red line appeared along its base. He stopped. He had been solving the problem he recognised instead of the problem that was there. He did not know yet that these were different problems."*

### The sitting down is the turning point
Every successful gate chapter has a moment where the solver stops all action. This must be preceded by the full exhaustion of wrong approaches. A sitting down that arrives after one wrong attempt is cheap. The reader must have run out of their own hypotheses before the sitting down lands.

The sitting down is physical. The prose names it directly — not as a technique but as a fact of the scene.

### The solution is quiet
After the right question arrives, the solution follows quickly and without drama. The gate closes with less ceremony than it opened. The emotional weight is in the question, not the answer.

**WRONG:**
*"With a triumphant surge of understanding, she placed the box in its slot and watched the gate dissolve in a cascade of light."*

**RIGHT:**
*"She put box twenty in slot twenty. The panel lit. The gate closed. That was all."*

## 3. TERMINOLOGY INTEGRATION ENGINE
*Operations are earned, not explained. This sequence applies to every Pólya operation.*

| Touch | Timing | Form | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | First occurrence | Character suffers the consequence of not having the operation. Gate punishes them through a specific wrong approach. No name attached yet. | The reader feels the cost before the concept has a name. |
| 1N | Same chapter or next | Name attached briefly after the physical event. One sentence maximum. Narrator voice. Not explained — just named. | Earn the term through felt consequence. |
| 2 | 2-4 chapters later | Character applies the operation to a new, different context — a gate of different type or ordinary life problem. No re-explanation. | Character owns the term. It is now a thinking tool. |
| 3 | 5-10 chapters later | Narrator uses naturally. No quotation marks. No explanation. Part of the world's description. | Term integrated into the world's language. |
| 4 | 10+ chapters later | Term combined with other earned terms. Reveals a deeper pattern neither term showed alone. | Term becomes a lens, not just a label. |

*Note on Touch 1 vs 1N: The experience always precedes the name. In early arcs the name may be withheld for one full chapter after the experience — the reader names it themselves before the story does. In later arcs the name may arrive in the same chapter, after the experience is complete.*

## 4. THREE DELIVERY CHANNELS
*Every operation introduction uses one of these three forms. Never more than one channel for the same operation in the same chapter.*

| Channel | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Narrator label | Operation just earned through failure. Attach the term briefly after the physical event. One sentence. | "She had spent forty minutes solving the wrong problem. She had never asked what the gate actually wanted. That was the mistake. It had a name, she would learn later." |
| Observer note | Mystery person's notebook. Precise. Observational. Never explanatory. | "Stood at threshold. Did not move. Four minutes. Asked what the room wanted before asking what the boxes were." |
| Character action | Operation becomes a thinking tool. Character applies it without being prompted, without naming it. | "He did not open the first file. He looked at the whole problem before touching any part of it." |

## 4B. WRITING THE MIRROR
*The mirror is the hardest element to write correctly. These rules govern every scene where gate mirror behaviour appears.*

### The core constraint: show the arrangement, not the meaning
The gate's mirror is physical. It is a reorganisation of the room's contents — light, objects, surfaces, spatial relationships. The prose describes what the room looks like. It never states what the room is showing. The reader who has learned to read registers will read the mirror. The reader who has not will experience the room as strange without knowing why. Both responses are correct.

**WRONG — stating the mirror's meaning:**
*"The room had arranged itself to reflect Kwame's assumption. The boxes were clustered by size, exactly as he had been sorting them, as if the gate were showing him that size was not the relevant property."*

**RIGHT — showing the arrangement:**
*"The boxes were in three groups now. He had not moved them all. The large ones were together. The medium ones. The small. He looked at the groups. He had done that. But the groups were more distinct than he had left them — edges aligned, spacing even. He had not done that."*

The reader asks: why is the room more organised than he left it? The mirror is operating. The prose does not answer. The reader holds the question.

### The mirror shifts when the approach shifts
When a solver changes wrong approach — or sits down — the room changes. This change is described physically. It is never attributed to the gate explicitly in early arcs. Later arcs may allow characters to notice the correlation without stating what it means.

**Early arc (Arc 1-2) — no attribution:**
*"She sat down. The room was different when she looked up. The light was not where it had been. She had been inside for forty minutes."*

**Later arc (Arc 4-5) — noticed but not named:**
*"He sat down. The room shifted — he had learned to expect this, though he could not have said when he had learned it. Something moved to the foreground. He looked at it the way he had learned to look: not at what it was, but at why it was now visible."*

### The mirror content must match the wrong approach type precisely
Every wrong approach type has a specific mirror content (see world_rules.md Section 5). The prose must render that specific content — not a generic "the room shifted." The executor's mirror emphasises the obvious pattern. The information gatherer's mirror gives completeness. These are different rooms. Write them as different rooms.

### The mirror in the sitting down is the pivot
The most important mirror moment is during the sitting down. This is when the mirror becomes readable — because the wrong approach has stopped and the solver is looking at the room rather than acting on it. The prose must show the solver's gaze changing: from operational (looking for what to do next) to structural (looking at what is there).

**The gaze change:**
*"She stopped sorting. She put the last box down. She looked at the room — not at the boxes but at the room. The way it was arranged. Who had arranged it like that. She had not. Or she had, and had not noticed. She looked at the part she had not touched."*

### Mystery person mirror notation
The mystery person reads mirror content directly. Their notebook records what the room showed, not what the solver did.

**Notebook format:**
- Gate grade.
- Approach type identified (executor / gatherer / etc).
- Mirror content: one precise physical description of what the room showed.
- Underlined: the gap — what the mirror showed was missing from the solver's frame.
- Register at sitting down. Register at right question. Time between.

**Example:**
*Grade 2. Specialist approach. Room organised by domain category — all chemical markers grouped, all mechanical elements grouped. Domain boundary visible as gap between groups. Underlined: gap between groups contained the unknown. Solver did not look at the gap. Register 2 at minute 31. Register 3 at minute 34. Three minutes from sitting down to right question.*

## 5. THE MYSTERY PERSON — VOICE RULES

The mystery person is never explained through their own words. They are described through what they do, where they go, what they notice. Their observations are precise. Their conclusions are never stated.

**The mystery person reads mirrors, not solvers.** Solver interiority is invisible. Gate mirror content is observable. The mystery person's notebook tracks what gates show — across many solvers, many cities, many grades. The pattern they are building is a pattern in what the gates keep reflecting. The reader, who has learned to read mirror content, can follow this pattern. Most characters cannot.

When the mystery person's perspective is shown: short. Observational. No interiority. They read the room's arrangement. They note what it showed. They note the gap. They move on.

**WRONG — observing solver behaviour:**
*"The observer watched the young woman stand still at the gate entrance. She was doing what so few did — looking before entering."*

**WRONG — register only, no mirror content:**
*"Threshold, four minutes. Gate held Register 1 until minute four. Shifted Register 2 at minute four, eleven seconds — before entry."*

**RIGHT — reading mirror content:**
*"Grade 1. Executor approach. Room emphasised the size sequence — boxes graduated, spacing increased with size. Obvious pattern made more obvious. Underlined: one box outside the sequence. Solver did not look at it. Register 2 at minute 22. Register 3 at minute 24."*

The mystery person's notebook entries follow this format:
- Gate grade.
- Approach type.
- Mirror content: one precise physical description of what the room showed.
- Underlined: the gap the mirror was showing that the solver missed or found.
- Register log: time-stamped transitions.
- Nothing else. No interpretation of what the gates mean overall.

The hidden coherence layer — what the mystery person is building toward, what the gates are communicating at the macro level — is NEVER present in prose. It exists only in mystery_anchor.json for planning purposes.

## 6. THE NEVER LIST
*Absolute prohibitions. If any appear in a draft, flag and revise before continuing.*

| Prohibition | Why |
|---|---|
| Breaking the fourth wall | The reader's hypotheses must be activated by prose, not instruction. Any direct address to the reader admits the prose is not doing its job. |
| A wrong approach that looks obviously wrong from the start | The reader must believe in the approach before it fails. Otherwise the failure teaches nothing. |
| Vague failure | "It didn't work" is not failure. Show exactly what happened when the wrong approach met this gate. |
| The sitting down arriving before wrong approaches are exhausted | A cheap sitting down produces a cheap right question. The reader must have run out of their own hypotheses first. |
| The right question arriving as a revelation | It arrives as a relief. Matter-of-fact. Not dramatic. The drama was in the failure sequence. |
| The solution being dramatic | The gate closes quietly. The emotional weight was in the question, not the answer. |
| The ordinary life echo being labelled as connected to the gate | Reader makes the connection. Never state it. Never have the character think "just like the gate." The echo scene must show at least one other person's involuntary response to the right question — someone going still, a conversation changing register. This is the ordinary life substitute for gate register behaviour. |
| The mystery person given interiority or explanation | Observable only. What they see, what they note. Never what they know or why. |
| An operation named before the cost has been felt | Name arrives after experience. Always. |
| Re-explaining an operation the character already owns | Use it naturally or do not use it. |
| Power substituting for understanding on any gate above Grade 1 | The physics of the world forbids this. |
| The gate explaining itself | Gates contain data. They do not label their own structure. The mirror shows. It does not point. Any sentence that states what the mirror is reflecting — rather than describing what the room looks like — is a violation. |
| More than two sentences connecting a physical experience to its label | One sentence maximum for the name attachment. |
| A technique succeeding perfectly the first time | No operation is demonstrated on first use without prior failure. |

## 7. FAILURE DRAMATISATION

*Wrong understanding produces physical, specific, visible consequences — not just abstract failure.*

| Layer | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Physical consequence | The gate does not respond in a specific, sensory way | "The panel went red. The sound it made was not a failure sound — it was a warning sound. There is a difference." |
| Effort cost | Time spent. Energy spent. The visible exhaustion of having worked hard in the wrong direction. | "She had been moving for forty minutes. Her arms were tired. She had nothing to show for it." |
| Confidence collapse | The moment the solver realises their approach was not wrong in execution but wrong in kind. | "He had been solving the problem he recognised instead of the problem that was there. He did not know yet that these were different problems." |
| Social consequence | Others observe. The cost of the wrong approach is visible to the world. | "Three people had not come out. The coordinator was out of options. That was the context when they let her in." |

## 8. CHARACTER LENS PROGRESSION

*Track how characters' vocabulary changes their perception at each arc. The narrator's voice shifts to reflect what they can now see. Operations accumulate — each arc adds to what prior arcs established.*

| Arc | Character's Fluency (cumulative) | What They See in Ordinary Life | Narrator's Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Naming the unknown before acting. Absence over presence. Separating parts of a condition. | Problems approached without first naming what is actually wanted. Things noticed missing rather than things present. | Names the physical markers (sitting down, hands flat, looking before touching) without naming the operation. |
| 2 | + Related problem. Did you use all the data? Looking back. | Every new problem has something it resembles. The unused piece of information that was always there. The habit of staying after to ask what happened. | Shows the unfocused gaze of someone searching memory rather than the room. The brief pause before filing something away. |
| 3 | + Analogy — simpler analogous problem. Decomposition. Auxiliary elements. Can you derive it differently? | Complex things can be approached through simpler things with the same structure. Not every part of a complex problem matters equally. Adding something not originally given to create a bridge. | Describes the moment a character mentally constructs a simpler version of something hard. Describes deliberate dropping of parts as natural. |
| 4 | + Auxiliary problem (invented). Specialisation. Can you use the result? | Invented detours as legitimate moves, not admissions of failure. The smallest specific case as a foothold. Results opening new problems rather than closing inquiry. | Describes the character choosing to set the original problem aside — not in defeat, but as a deliberate move. Names this as distinct from giving up. |
| 5 | + Working backwards. Generalisation. Inventor's Paradox. Reductio ad absurdum. | Starting from the end rather than the beginning. Making a problem bigger to make it easier. Assuming the opposite to find the contradiction. | Describes the physical reversal — turning away from the data side — as a habit. Shows the counter-intuitive expansion without labelling it paradoxical. |
| 6 | + Variation / restatement. | The stated problem is often not the real problem. The version of a problem that cannot be solved is not the same as the problem. | Contrasts characters who restate with those who execute on the wrong problem. Both visible in same chapter. The narrator does not name who is right. |
| 7 | + Heuristic vs proof. Subconscious work. | The difference between following a path and knowing the path is correct. The right question arriving after stopping. | Shows a character acting on provisional reasoning while tracking separately what remains unverified. Shows the return after stopping — the right question present. |
| 8 | + Auxiliary problem chains. Synthesis. | A sequence of smaller problems, each leading to the next, and the path back through them. | Describes the chain forward and the synthesis back as two distinct acts, both visible in a character's approach to a complex ordinary problem. |
| Mystery person | All operations as instinct, applied to the macro problem. | The gates as a communication. The macro problem correctly stated. | Shows what a person looks like when the full process is internalised. The reader has been building toward this since Arc 1. The mystery person's notebook, read in full, is an auxiliary problem chain applied to the question: what are the gates really asking? |
