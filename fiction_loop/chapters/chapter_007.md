# CHAPTER 007 — ARC TRANSITION

The report came in at 08:47 Accra time. Yaw Boateng read it twice. Then he read it again.

Three solvers dead on Grade 2 gates in the last four days. Two of them had closed Grade 1 gates without help. The third had been part of the first cohort to go through the new accelerated training. None of their gate-logs showed what went wrong.

Yaw put the tablet down. He stood and walked to the map on the wall. Pins marked every active gate on the continent. Red for unresolved. Green for closed. Yellow for in progress. He looked at the red pins. There were more than there had been last week.

He called the Nairobi office.

"It's the same pattern," he said when someone picked up. "The ones who closed their first gate fast—they're the ones dying now."

The voice on the other end was quiet. "We're seeing it here too."

"What do the logs say?"

"Same as yours. Method applied correctly. Gate didn't close. Solver kept working. Then the gate went dark."

"No warning?"

"None that we can read."

Yaw looked at the map again. He didn't say anything for a long moment.

"Maybe we're training them wrong," he said finally.

No one on the call had an answer.

—

In Lagos, Yejide Adeyemi walked the inspection floor at the fabric mill. She moved between the long tables, her hands quiet at her sides. The morning shift had been running for two hours. The bolts were coming through at the usual rate.

She stopped at the third table. The checker there was working fast, marking flaws as the fabric passed under the light. Yejide watched for thirty seconds.

Then she said, "What's missing?"

The checker looked up. "Nothing. The count is fine."

Yejide didn't answer. She pointed at the roll that had just passed through. The checker reversed it. Nothing obvious.

"Look at the pattern," Yejide said. "You're looking for what's there. Look for what's not there."

The checker frowned, then went still. After a moment she said, "The repeat is off. Not in the print—in the weave. That's why the count looked right."

Yejide nodded once. She moved on to the next table.

She did not think about gates. She had not thought about the Lagos fabric-inspection gate since she walked out of it. She did not name what she was doing. It was just how she looked at fabric now.

—

In Accra, Kwabena Asante opened the morning dispatch log and scanned the columns. The Achimota depot had been running three shifts since the gates started opening near industrial zones. The logistics were straining.

He saw it in the first thirty seconds.

The Tuesday morning routing had a gap. Not a missing entry—a pattern that didn't close. Three vehicles assigned to the same quadrant, one vehicle assigned to the adjacent quadrant, but the adjacent quadrant had a delivery point that was sixteen kilometres inside the boundary of the first.

Kwabena didn't call anyone. He opened the map overlay, checked the addresses, confirmed what he already knew. The routing algorithm had treated the quadrants as fixed zones. It had not accounted for the shape of the delivery point.

He reassigned one vehicle from the saturated quadrant to cover the outlier. The total distance dropped by fourteen kilometres.

He updated the sheet and closed it.

He did not think about the Achimota gate. He had not thought about it in weeks. The dispatch problem felt familiar. He didn't ask why.

—

The guild's internal review convened at 14:00. Eight senior coordinators from eight hubs. The call was audio-only. No one wanted to look at faces.

"We've isolated thirty-seven Grade 2 closures so far," said the Nairobi lead. "Of those, twenty-two were closed by solvers who had no formal guild training."

"What's the sample size?" Yaw asked.

"Forty-six total closures on Grade 2. The rest were guild-trained."

"And the mortality?"

"Twelve deaths. All guild-trained. None of the non-guild."

The line was silent.

"That's not possible," someone said.

"It's the data."

"The non-guild ones—what are they doing differently?"

"We don't know. We can't track them until they register a gate entry. Most of them don't. They just walk in and walk out."

Yaw rubbed his eyes. "We need to find them."

"We're trying. But they're not obvious. They're not coming to us. They're just—surviving."

—

The new gate opened in Dakar at 16:22.

It was Grade 2. The guild had a team on standby less than a kilometre away. They arrived within seven minutes.

Kuuku Dadzie was the first to enter. He had trained at the Accra guildhall for six weeks. He had closed two Grade 1 gates. He knew the method: enter, orient, catalogue, test the obvious. Do not rush. Do not assume.

The gate room was small. A single space, maybe four metres square. Against the far wall, a low table. On the table: five objects. A glass jar half-filled with water. A metal ruler. A book, closed. A small wooden cube. A length of blue string coiled in a circle.

The walls were bare. The floor was stone. There was no other furniture.

Kuuku stood at the entrance for three seconds. Then he moved to the table.

He picked up the ruler. Checked its length. Standard thirty centimetres. He put it down.

He picked up the book. Read the cover. It was a pocket dictionary, French to Wolof. He opened it. The pages were intact. He closed it and set it back.

He picked up the wooden cube. It was smooth. No markings. He turned it over. Solid.

He touched the string. Regular cotton. The coil was loose, not knotted.

He looked at the jar. The water level was about halfway. He lifted it. The glass was cool. He put it down.

He stepped back and looked at the table again.

He didn't see a pattern.

He catalogued again. Five objects. No hidden panels. No instructions. The gate didn't shift.

Kuuku tried a different arrangement. He moved the ruler to the right of the jar. He put the book next to the cube. He uncoiled the string and stretched it in a straight line from the jar to the edge of the table.

The gate did nothing.

He waited. The guild protocol said: if the gate doesn't respond to reorganisation, check for a trigger. He checked the underside of the table. Smooth. He examined the walls. No seams.

He came back to the table and looked at the objects one by one.

The water in the jar was still. The ruler was straight. The book was closed. The cube was solid. The string was blue.

He had never seen a gate like this. But the method said: when you don't see the pattern, go back to what you know. He went back to what he knew. He tried to match it to a Grade 1 gate he had closed. That gate had been about ordering. He tried ordering the objects by size. No response. By material. No response. By function—measuring, containing, writing, playing, tying. Nothing.

He tried one last thing. He placed the cube inside the jar. It sank. He took it out and dried it.

The gate did nothing.

Kuuku stood very still for a long time. He could feel the wall clock in his mind. The gate wouldn't wait forever. He had been inside eleven minutes.

He looked once more at the table.

He couldn't see what he was missing.

At twelve minutes, the gate's light flickered once. Kuuku stepped back toward the entrance. He waited three more seconds. Nothing.

He turned and walked out.

The safety coordinator met him at the perimeter. "Well?"

Kuuku shook his head.

He didn't say anything else. He walked to the observation tent and sat down. He wrote nothing in his log.

In the guild's data room an hour later, the new entry appeared: Gate 477-D, Grade 2, Dakar. Attempted. Unsolved. Solver: Dadzie, K. Method: standard catalogue and reorganisation. No closure.

No one noticed that the objects on the table had not been chosen at random. Three of them—the jar, the ruler, the book—shared a property that was invisible if you handled them one at a time. The property was not in the objects themselves. It was in the relationship between them. The same relationship had appeared in a Grade 1 gate in Kumasi two weeks earlier. Kuuku had closed that gate. He had not thought to reach for it.

The Dakar gate remained open for another hour. Then a second solver entered. A woman whose name the guild did not have. She had no training. She stood at the entrance for twenty seconds, looking at the room before she moved. Then she sat down on the stone floor.

She did not touch the objects. She looked at them. Not one by one—the whole table. Her gaze moved slowly, as if reading something.

After three minutes she stood up. She walked to the table. She moved the jar to the centre. She placed the ruler and the book on either side, parallel. She uncoiled the string and wrapped it once around the jar's neck. She set the cube on top of the book.

The gate shifted. The light steadied. The far wall dissolved, and the entrance behind her widened.

She walked through. The gate closed.

The guild would file the closure as "unidentified civilian." They would note it in the statistics that showed non-guild solvers closing gates the guild could not. They would not see her sit down. They would not know that what she did looked nothing like guild training.

They would not know that she had closed a gate in Kumasi, two weeks earlier, alone.

—

In the archives at the Accra guildhall, a junior clerk was filing field reports. She opened a folder labelled "Unidentified Documents—Gates." Inside were five torn pages.

She spread them on her desk. Three of them were the same size, torn from the same kind of notebook. The handwriting was small, precise. Each page recorded a gate event. Solver approaches. Times down to the second. Observations on what the solvers missed.

The pages were not official. No one had claimed them. They had been found inside gates—one in Achimota, one in Kumasi, two in Lagos, one in Dakar.

The clerk read them slowly. She didn't understand most of it. But she noticed that the same three words appeared on every page: *executor*, *system builder*, *information gatherer*. She had never seen those terms in guild training.

She photocopied the pages. She sent the copies to the senior coordinator's office with a note: *Found in multiple gates. Origin unknown.*

The copies went into a file. The file went onto a shelf. No one opened it again.

—

In Lagos, Yejide finished her shift and walked out into the evening. She didn't hurry. She had stopped hurrying since the gate.

In Accra, Kwabena locked the dispatch office and walked to the bus stop. He noticed the bus was three minutes late. He didn't check his watch. He had stopped checking his watch.

In Nairobi, Wanjiku Mwangi logged out of the hospital records system. She had spent the afternoon tracing a discrepancy in the surgical inventory. The error was not in the count. The error was in how the count had been organised. She had found it by ignoring the categories and looking at the dates. She went home and slept better than she had in months.

In Dakar, Fatou Ndiaye filed the land registry update that had been sitting on her desk for two days. The boundary dispute was resolved. She had not argued about the lines. She had asked what was missing from the original survey. The missing part had been a water access point that predated both claimants. She had found it in the colonial records. She closed the file and turned off her lamp.

None of them knew each other. None of them could have named what they had learned.

The arc closed.

The next gate opened at dawn.
