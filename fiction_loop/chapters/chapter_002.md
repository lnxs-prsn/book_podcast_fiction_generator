# CHAPTER 002

Kwabena walked the north wall of the Achimota depot. The morning air smelled of diesel and damp earth. He held his clipboard. He checked the bay numbers. He marked his paper. 

The brickwork rippled. The mortar dissolved into grey light. A doorway opened in the solid wall. 

Kwabena stopped. He looked at the opening. 

Ama Serwah walked up behind him. She carried a tray of roasted plantain. Mr. Owusu followed. He held a broom. 

"What is that?" Ama asked. 

Kwabena did not answer. He stepped through the threshold. Ama stepped in after him. Mr. Owusu followed. 

The room was large. It smelled of old paper and ozone. The walls were lined with wooden slots. Each slot had a brass number plate. On the floor lay hundreds of route manifests. They were printed on thick yellow card. 

Kwabena looked at the manifests. He looked at the slots. The numbers on the slots went from one to fifty. The manifests had route numbers printed in black ink. 

He moved. He picked up a manifest. Route 12. He walked to slot 12. He slid it in. 

He picked up another. Route 4. Slot 4. 

He worked fast. His hands were steady. He gathered armfuls of the yellow cards. He matched them to the brass numbers. He walked back and forth across the concrete floor. His shoes made a sharp sound with every step. 

Ama watched him. Mr. Owusu leaned on his broom. 

Kwabena looked at the wall. The manifests he had placed were perfectly aligned. The edges of the yellow cards were flush. The spacing between them was exactly equal. He had not measured the spacing. The pattern was clean. It was almost too easy to read. 

He picked up the next manifest. Route 33. He walked to slot 33. 

The wall did not change. The light in the room remained flat. The doorway behind them did not close. 

He placed the card in the slot. He looked at the wall. The cards were still perfectly aligned. The spacing was still exact. The obvious pattern was complete. 

The gate held. 

Kwabena picked up another card. He looked at the number. He looked at the wall. The pattern was finished. There was nowhere else to put them. 

"It is not just the numbers," Ama said. She set her plantain tray on a wooden bench. She knelt on the floor. 

She picked up a stack of manifests. "These are all for the eastbound routes. The drivers are different." 

She began to sort them. She made a pile for Kwame. A pile for Yaw. A pile for Kofi. She arranged the piles in neat rows. She checked the fare printed on each card. She sub-sorted them by price. 

Mr. Owusu watched her. "You are making a table," he said. 

"I am finding the order," Ama said. 

She worked methodically. She touched every card. She assigned every card to a category. She placed the piles near the wall. She matched the driver piles to the wall slots. 

Kwabena watched the room. 

The walls had changed. The wooden slots were grouped. The first ten slots held the eastbound manifests. The next ten held the westbound. The categorization was perfect. Every card was in a pile. Every pile was straight. The room was entirely ordered. 

Ama sat back on her heels. She looked at her rows. She looked at the wall. 

The light did not shift. The air did not move. The doorway remained open. 

Mr. Owusu looked at the wall. He looked at Ama’s rows. He looked at Kwabena. 

Kwabena felt a tightness in his chest. He had matched the numbers. Ama had sorted the data. The room was ordered. The gate did not care. 

He stopped moving. He let his arms drop to his sides. 

He looked at the room. He did not look at the manifests. He did not look at the slots. He looked at the space between them. He looked at the walls. He looked at the floor. 

He walked to a wooden crate near the center of the room. He sat down. 

He put his hands flat on his knees. He kept his hands still. 

The room changed. 

The light shifted. It came from the ceiling now, not the walls. The shadows moved. The perfect rows of manifests on the floor blurred at the edges. The neat alignment on the wall lost its sharpness. The background became the foreground. 

Kwabena watched the room. He did not reach for anything. He just looked at what was there. 

He saw the wall. He saw the slots. He saw the manifests. 

He saw the brass plate above slot one. It did not say ‘1’. It said ‘Weight’. 

He looked at the manifests in the slots. They did not just have route numbers. They had a second line of text. ‘Cargo’. 

He looked at the floor. The condition was written on the doorframe, in small letters. ‘The load must balance the route.’ 

Kwabena stood up. He did not rush. 

He held up his left hand. He extended his thumb. 

"We need the departure weight," he said. His voice was quiet. 

He extended his index finger. 

"We have the route manifests and the cargo lists." 

He extended his middle finger. 

"The condition is the balance." 

He looked at the wall. The light in the room brightened. The shadows retreated. The air felt lighter. The gate had shifted before he moved. 

He walked to the wall. He did not look at the route numbers. He looked at the cargo lists. He picked up the manifest for Route 12. He read the weight. He walked to the scale on the floor. He placed the manifest on the scale. He adjusted the brass weights until the scale balanced. 

He did this for every slot. He did not rush. He matched the weight to the balance. 

When he placed the last manifest on the scale, the wall hummed. The light faded. The brickwork reformed. The mortar returned. The doorway was gone. 

The room was just a storage room at the depot. The manifests were just paper. 

Ama picked up her plantain tray. Mr. Owusu picked up his broom. 

Kwabena looked at the floor near the threshold. 

A small piece of paper lay on the concrete. It was torn from a black notebook. 

He picked it up. The handwriting was small, precise, and unfamiliar. 

*Grade 2. Executor approach. Room arranged the visible route data into a cleaner sequence — edges aligned, spacing even, the obvious pattern made more obvious. Underlined: the solver never named what was wanted before acting. Register 2 at minute 33. Register 3 at minute 36. Three minutes from sitting down to right question.* 

Kwabena read the words. He did not know what they meant. He folded the paper. He put it in his pocket. 

***

Later that morning, Kwabena sat at his desk in the dispatch office. The fan turned overhead. It made a low, rhythmic clicking sound. 

The depot manager, Mr. Mensah, stood by the filing cabinet. The roster clerk, Esi, sat across from Kwabena. 

"Bus 404 is missing from the afternoon roster," Mr. Mensah said. "Esi says it is assigned to the Tema route. The driver says it is in the yard. We need to find out who made the error." 

Esi opened the first file on the desk. She pulled out the assignment sheet. "I will check the sign-out log." 

Kwabena looked at the file. He looked at the assignment sheet. He looked at Mr. Mensah. 

He did not open the first file. He put his hands flat on the desk. He looked at the desk. He looked at the files. 

"What are we actually trying to find?" Kwabena asked. 

Esi stopped. Her hands hovered over the file. She did not pull the next page. 

Mr. Mensah stopped talking. He looked at Kwabena. The room was very quiet. The fan turned overhead. 

"We need to know where the bus is," Mr. Mensah said. His voice was slower. 

"No," Kwabena said. "We need to know why the driver thinks it is in the yard when the log says it is on the road. The bus is not the missing thing. The signature is." 

Esi closed the file. She pushed it away. She looked at the sign-out log. 

"Let me check the driver’s signature," she said.
