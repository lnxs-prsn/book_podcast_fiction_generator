# CHAPTER 004

Wanjiru Muthoni stood at the entrance to the gate. She did not know it was a gate yet.

She had been walking home from the hospital depot, her bag heavy with the day’s paperwork. Nairobi traffic hummed behind her. Ahead, the alley between two shuttered shops shimmered oddly in the late sun. She slowed. The air smelled of wet concrete and diesel. Nothing moved. She stepped forward.

The alley folded shut behind her.

Now she stood in a room lit by overhead panels that gave no glare. The floor was smooth grey stone. Against one wall sat three wooden crates. Each bore a stencilled label: MEDICAL, LAB, GENERAL. A clipboard rested on top of the middle crate. On it, a single line of text:

*Deliver only if temperature-controlled, sealed, and accompanied by Form 7B.*

Wanjiru frowned. She knew this phrasing. Her supervisor used it all the time—three conditions compressed into one sentence, as if they were inseparable. She picked up the clipboard. The paper felt real. She turned toward the crates.

A man in a dark blue uniform entered from the opposite side. He did not look at her. He went straight to the crates, lifted the lid of the first, and began sorting its contents onto the floor. His movements were fast, sure. He grouped vials by size, then syringes by length, then bandages by roll diameter. He worked without pause. The room stayed quiet.

After ten minutes, he stopped. He looked at the clipboard. He read the line again. He tried to lift the crate marked MEDICAL. It did not budge. He pushed. He pulled. He braced his foot against the wall and heaved. The crate remained fixed to the floor.

He left without speaking. The door behind him clicked shut.

Wanjiru watched him go. She stepped closer to the crates. She opened the MEDICAL crate herself. Inside: vials, syringes, gauze pads, gloves. All standard issue. She checked seals. All intact. She touched the walls of the crate. Cool, but not cold. No refrigeration unit inside.

She closed the lid. She moved to the LAB crate. Same contents, slightly different arrangement. GENERAL held cleaning supplies, spare uniforms, a stack of clipboards like the one she held.

She returned to the clipboard. *Temperature-controlled, sealed, and accompanied by Form 7B.* She had seen Form 7B before—it was a customs slip, rarely required for internal transfers. She did not have it. But neither condition alone seemed sufficient to block delivery. Taken together, they formed a knot she could not untie.

Another solver entered. Older. Carrying a leather satchel. He set it down carefully. He took out a notebook, a pen, a pair of thin gloves. He walked the perimeter of the room, noting dimensions, light sources, crate positions. He opened each crate slowly, recorded every item, checked labels, measured gaps between objects. He spent twenty minutes documenting. Only then did he approach the clipboard.

He read the line. He compared it to his notes. He verified that none of the crates carried Form 7B. He confirmed that none showed active cooling. He noted that all seals were intact. He wrote a summary paragraph. Then he attempted to slide the MEDICAL crate sideways. It did not move. He tried lifting it by the handles. Nothing. He tested the floor beneath it for hinges or locks. Found none.

He packed his things. Left without looking back.

Wanjiru remained. She walked to the center of the room. She looked at the crates. She looked at the clipboard. She read the sentence again.

*Deliver only if temperature-controlled, sealed, and accompanied by Form 7B.*

She read it once more. Then she covered the last two clauses with her thumb. She read only: *Deliver only if temperature-controlled.*

She went to the MEDICAL crate. She pressed her palm flat against its side. Still cool, but not actively chilled. She opened it. No ice packs. No coolant gel. Just room-temperature medical supplies.

She uncovered the next clause. Covered the first and last. *Deliver only if sealed.* She checked every seal again. All intact.

She uncovered the last clause. Covered the first two. *Deliver only if accompanied by Form 7B.* No form present.

She stood still. She let her hand drop to her side. She looked not at the crates, but at the space between them. At the clipboard. At the sentence as a whole.

Then she reached into her bag and pulled out a blank sheet of paper. She wrote three lines:

1. Temperature-controlled?  
2. Sealed?  
3. Form 7B present?

She checked off number two. She circled number one. She stared at it.

She walked to the MEDICAL crate. She lifted the lid. She removed a single vial. She held it in her hand. She walked to the far wall, where a small panel glowed faintly blue. She placed the vial against it.

The panel warmed under her fingers. Light spread through the vial’s glass. Condensation formed on its surface. The vial was now cold.

She returned it to the crate. She closed the lid. She stepped back.

The crate rose an inch off the floor. A soft chime sounded. The door across the room opened.

She walked out.

Outside, the Nairobi evening had deepened. Streetlights flickered on. The alley was just an alley again. She walked home without looking back.

---

Three days later, Wanjiru sat at her desk in the hospital supply depot. Rain tapped the corrugated roof. Her supervisor’s checklist lay before her:

*Approve shipment only if vendor-certified, palletised, and stamped with current expiry.*

She had stalled on it for two days. Every time she tried to process the delivery, something blocked her. The vendor certification was missing one signature. The pallets were stacked but not strapped. The expiry stamp was faded but legible.

She stared at the line. She took a fresh sheet of paper. She wrote:

1. Vendor-certified?  
2. Palletised?  
3. Current expiry stamp?

She checked the shipment against each line separately. The pallets were fine. The stamp was acceptable. The missing signature was the only hard stop.

She called the vendor. Got the signature emailed within ten minutes. Approved the shipment. Moved to the next file.

Across the room, her colleague David paused mid-sentence. He had been arguing with logistics about another stalled delivery. He stopped talking. He looked at Wanjiru. Then he turned back to his screen and began rewriting his own checklist in three separate lines.

---

The man in the grey coat stood across the street during the system builder’s attempt. He watched the solver categorise every object, assign each to a quadrant, label bins with precise handwriting. The room reorganised itself around the method—drawers slid open to receive sorted items, shelves aligned to match the solver’s grid, labels appeared where none had been.

The man opened his black notebook. He wrote:

*Grade 2. System builder approach. Room mirrored perfect categorisation — every item assigned, every category named, spatial order imposed. Underlined: the clipboard remained untouched throughout. Register 2 at minute 18. Register 3 never reached.*

He closed the book. Walked away.

Later, after Wanjiru exited the gate, he stood near the bus stop. A woman waiting for transport glanced at him, then at the alley. “Did you see her come out?” she asked.

He nodded.

“Calm?” she said.

“Yes,” he said.

He boarded the next bus. Did not look back.
