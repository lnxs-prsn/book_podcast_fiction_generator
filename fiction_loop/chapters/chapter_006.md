# CHAPTER 006

The door was steel, set into a concrete wall behind the old Iganmu bus depot. Yejide stood at the threshold longer than she had the first time. Not out of fear. The room inside was large and lit from somewhere above, and three people were already inside, moving.

She stepped through.

The gate room smelled of starch and indigo and the faint mineral tang of uncured dye. Bolts of fabric stood on wooden pallets in the centre — ankara, wax print, a heavy roll of plain cotton. A metal inspection table ran the length of one wall. Above it, a row of brass hooks held sample tags. One hook was empty. A clipboard with a delivery manifest lay on the table, half-filled. Everything else — the bolts, the pallets, the lighting panels — whispered *factory floor*, the kind of room Yejide had spent ten years inside. She knew the shape of this place before she had looked at it fully.

She did not move immediately.

A young man was already working. He had the fast hands of someone who fixes things by touching them. He was pulling bolts off pallets, matching their end-caps to the sample tags on the hooks — blue to blue, yellow to yellow, the heavy cotton to a tag marked *cotton — unbleached*. He moved with the certainty of a man who trusts patterns. He had not looked at the whole room. He had walked in and gone to the colour that was wrong.

Yejide watched.

The room began to reorganise itself around his motion. The blue bolts shifted where he had placed them, their end-caps aligning more perfectly than any human hand could manage. The yellow pile tightened. The tags on the hooks swung gently and then stilled, arranged by colour — all the blue-tagged hooks together, the white ones together. The pattern he was following had become visible.

He saw it. He smiled, the smile of someone who knows what he is doing.

The empty hook did not move. It stayed between the last blue-tagged hook and the first white-tagged one — a metal curve with nothing on it. It did not recede. It did not fill. It simply waited.

He finished matching. He stepped back. He looked at the gate.

Nothing.

The light panels did not dim. The door did not appear. The gate held Register 1 — inert, undisturbed, as if no one had touched it. The young man’s hands dropped to his sides. He stood there, breathing, not understanding. The room stayed exactly as he had arranged it. The empty hook remained.

A woman stepped forward before he could try again. She was older, her grey hair pulled tight, and she carried a method. She set her bag on the floor and began to work through the room with the patience of someone who has done this before. She did not match by colour. She read the manifest. She cross-referenced supplier codes, batch numbers, dye lots. She laid bolts on the floor in a grid — ankara here, wax print there, plain cotton in its own section. She labelled the grid in her mind, muttering under her breath.

The room responded. The light panels above each section of her grid flickered — Register 1, then a brief false Register 3, a pulse that looked like the gate was waking. The bolts settled into her categories as if they had always belonged there. The tags on the hooks rearranged themselves by supplier code, alphabetical, precise. The grid was perfect. The room was the most organized version of itself it had ever been.

The empty hook sat in its cell of the grid — a space in the wax-print section, an absence with a label she had assigned: *item unmatched*.

She finished. She looked up at the gate, expecting it to close.

It did not. The flicker died. The grid held. The gate was elsewhere. She turned away, her jaw tight.

The third man had been standing near the clipboard since the beginning, writing. He was tall and thin, a newcomer Yejide had never seen before. He wore a faded workshirt with a pen clipped to the pocket, and his eyes moved across the room the way an auditor’s move — cataloguing, logging, never asking what the data was for. He did not touch anything. He recorded the bolts by count and dimension, the tags by code and location, the empty hook by its position on the wall. He wrote: *hook 4 — empty*. He turned the page and continued.

The room gave him everything he asked for. The manifest became fully readable under his gaze — every entry crisp, every number legible. The bolts held still. The tags did not shift. The room was a perfect catalogue, complete and patient.

He finished. He closed his notebook and waited.

The gate did not move. It held Register 1, undisturbed — as if no one had entered. The man looked at his list, then at the empty hook, as though searching for a column he had forgotten to add. He did not ask the question.

The room was still.

Yejide sat down on an empty pallet. She was not tired. She had been standing for nearly thirty minutes, and she had watched three people exhaust themselves doing what she had done the first time. She knew the feeling of that — hands moving, mind chasing a pattern, the gate refusing. She did not know the word for it, but her body did.

She looked at the room. Not at the bolts or the tags or the grid the woman had left on the floor. At the room. At the way it had arranged itself while they were working — the blue-tagged hooks together, the grid still holding its shape, the manifest lying open. At the empty hook between the blue and the white, the metal curve with nothing on it, the space that three people had seen and catalogued or matched around or filed as an anomaly but had never asked about.

She stood up.

Her feet took her to the wall, not the table. She passed the bolts. She passed the grid. She stopped in front of the empty hook. Her hand lifted and rested on it — fingers curling around the brass curve, palm open to the space where something should have been. Her eyes moved from the hook to the manifest on the table, and she read the list from the back of the room, not touching it. She found the line without walking over — *indigo sample, supplier Oke-Aro, lot 41* — and there was no tag for it on the hooks. No bolt of indigo fabric in the room. The manifest said it should be here. It was not.

She said, very quietly, “It’s missing.”

The gate shifted. Register 2 had been holding while she sat — a quality of light that was not quite waiting, but had stopped ignoring her. Now it deepened. The empty hook under her hand grew warm, and the light panels over the pallets dimmed except for one, which brightened over a bolt of heavy navy fabric she had not seen before, tucked behind the plain cotton. It was not indigo. It was the wrong colour. But when she walked to it and lifted the cut edge, the tag sewn inside read *Oke-Aro, lot 41*.

Register 3. The room opened. The air changed — less dense, as if a pressure had released. The door she had come through reappeared in the far wall, its edges glowing faintly.

The young man turned. The woman with the grid looked up. The auditor closed his notebook and stared at the bolt Yejide held, as though it had violated his inventory.

Yejide set the bolt on the inspection table. She did not explain. She walked toward the door, and the room dimmed behind her.

On the table, beside the manifest, lay a torn notebook page. She had not seen it before. She picked it up.

---

*Gate G-006 (Grade 2)*  
*Entrants: four.*  
*Approach A (executive pattern-match) — Register 1 throughout. Room sorts by colour; hook 4 remains empty, not receding.*  
*Approach B (grid taxonomy) — Register 1 with false 3 flicker on completion. Room mirrors categorical grid; hook 4 sits in cell “unmatched”.*  
*Approach C (full audit) — Register 1 undisturbed. Room yields complete catalogue; item 4 listed as “empty”, no query.*  
*Timeout at 14:02:17 — no closure.*  
*Approach D (threshold-standing, gaze-to-gaps) — Register 2 at sit. Hand on hook 4 at 14:03:51. Latch at 14:04:03. Gate closed 14:04:05.*  
*— Gap: absence unnamed by A, B, C. Named by D.*  

Yejide read it twice. She folded the page and put it in her pocket. The door was open. The other three were already gone. She stepped through into the diesel-smelling air of the bus depot and walked toward the junction where the danfo would stop.

---

The desk was new. It had been a floor supervisor’s desk once, but Mrs. Okafor had moved to the mezzanine and the cubicle by the inspection bay had stood empty for two months. Now Yejide’s name was on the glass. Her workstation held a monitor, a metal tray of fabric swatches, and a stack of quality reports flagged for review.

The floor supervisor appeared before she had finished her tea.

“Line three is down,” Mrs. Okafor said. “The indigo run. Bolts are coming out with shading bands every forty centimetres. QC’s been at it since morning.”

Yejide walked to line three. The inspection table there was a disaster: dye-lot reports, supplier manifests, swatches spread in a fan, cross-referencing notes scribbled on scrap cardboard. Two junior inspectors were arguing about whether the fault was in the base cotton or the dye bath. The woman from the dye lab was holding a spectrometer reading and shaking her head. Everyone had a piece of data.

Yejide did not pick up the first report. She stood at the end of the table, her hands flat on the metal surface, and she looked at the whole problem the way she had learned to look at things — from the entrance, before moving. Her eyes moved past the reports, past the swatches, past the litany of what was present. She searched for what should be here and was not.

The floor supervisor, halfway through another sentence, went still.

Yejide said, “The cotton from Ikorodu mill was due Tuesday.”

The dye-lab woman lowered the spectrometer. One of the junior inspectors stopped mid-argument and reached for a binder on the shelf — the delivery schedule. He flipped pages. The room held quiet.

“It hasn’t come,” he said.

Mrs. Okafor exhaled. “They’ve been blending with whatever stock we had. The lot was short.”

Yejide nodded. She did not need to say more. The floor supervisor was already dialling the mill. The dye-lab woman began packing her equipment. The problem was not the dye or the fabric — it was the absence of the right cotton from the start. The line would stay down until the shipment arrived. Now they knew.

Yejide walked back to her cubicle. The metal tray of swatches was still waiting. She picked up the top one — a wax-print sample with an uneven repeat — and began to note the defect codes. Her hand was steady. Outside the window, the freight yard was moving, trucks pulling in with cotton bales from somewhere inland.
