# CHAPTER 001

Yejide left the house before the sun came up. Fadeke stood in the doorway. Her hands were full of papers. She held them against her chest like protection.

"You didn't sign the form," Fadeke said.

"I signed the work log," Yejide said. She stepped past her sister. Her slippers slapped the wet pavement. The air smelled of exhaust and rain.

Fadeke followed her to the gate. "The registration deadline is Friday. If you don't pay the fee—"

"I paid my market toll today." Yejide climbed onto the bus. She found a seat near the window. She put her head against the glass. The engine shook her spine. She closed her eyes.

The bus stopped in traffic. A jam of horns. People moved outside the windows. She looked out. Across the street, a man stood under a tree. He wore a grey coat. He watched the traffic. He watched the passengers. Yejide saw him for three seconds. Then the bus lurched forward. She did not remember his face. She remembered the position. He did not move.

The gate appeared at the next stop.

It did not knock. It just filled the space where the pavement ended. A rectangular mouth in the city. Three people stood near the entrance. One man in a blue shirt. One woman in a suit. One younger man in cycling gear. They had not seen it. Yejide had walked past the exit. She turned back.

The door was not locked. She pushed inside.

The room was concrete. Cold floor. High ceiling. On the far wall, a grid of shallow stone niches. Five rows. Five columns. Twenty-five spaces total.

Twenty-four contained carved wooden birds. They were different sizes. Different shapes. Some had wings spread. Some had heads tuckfiction_loop/chapters/chapter_001.mded. The last niche was empty. It was a dark rectangle in the center of the third row.

The young man in cycling gear spoke first. "Is this a game?"

The man in the blue shirt ignored him. He stepped forward. He raised his hands. He touched the birds.

This was the executor. He worked by touch. By sequence. He took a bird from the left corner. He placed it next to another. He aligned the beaks. He sorted the flock by wing length.

He moved fast. His breath came in short bursts. He did not speak. He organized the surface.

The room reacted. The light shifted. It pulled tight toward the group he touched. The shadows between the birds grew sharp. The grain of the wood became visible. The pattern he built—the line of increasing size—became distinct. More legible than before. He could see the order clearly now.

He waited for the door to open behind him. It stayed shut.

"This isn't right," the cyclist said.

"Not yet," the man in the blue shirt said. He kept arranging. He finished the row. He smoothed the spacing. The light hummed. Nothing moved.

A woman entered then. She carried a black binder. She wore the posture of someone who expected rules. She looked at the wall. She opened the binder. She pulled out a pen.

This was the system builder. She worked by category. She checked the wood species. Oak. Mahogany. Teak. She checked the carving style. Traditional. Modern. Abstract. She wrote the data in lines.

She spoke her findings aloud. "Group A. North-West quadrant."

The room changed. The birds seemed to slide slightly. Those of the same wood density drifted together. Those of similar carving technique grouped apart. The walls of the niches aligned to form boundaries. Chemical markers here. Mechanical elements there. The categorisation was perfect. Every element processed. Assigned. Stored.

The woman looked at her list. It was complete. She looked at the door. It remained closed.

She tapped her pen. "Did you miss a variable?"

"I think we need more data," the cyclist said.

The cyclist went to the wall. He pointed at the first bird. "Wood type number one."

He pointed at the second. "Wood type number two."

He pointed at the twelfth. "Bird twelve. Wing span four inches."

He catalogued the whole room. He did not ask questions. He listed facts. His voice was flat. He wanted the record to be true.

The room responded. The light flattened. Everything looked equally lit. The shadows vanished. The birds looked like they belonged in a museum case. Every detail was available. There were no secrets in the grain. No hidden marks. The catalogue was complete.

The cyclist stopped. He counted the list. "Twenty-four entries."

"Check the empty one," the blue-shirt man said.

"The empty one has no entries," the cyclist said. He waited for the door.

It did not open.

They turned to Yejide. She had been standing near the door. Her shoes were dirty. Her uniform bag hung on her shoulder. She felt the heat of their frustration. It pressed against her neck.

"I check fabric," Yejide said. "I look for holes."

The blue-shirt man nodded. "Look."

Yejide stepped forward. She knew the routine. Find the defect. Fix the thread. Replace the weave. She approached the wall. She leaned in. She ran a finger along the rim of the niches. Smooth stone. Dust settled in the corners.

She inspected the birds. She looked for cracks. Chips. Glue lines. None existed. The craftsmanship was high. The paint held. The joints were tight.

She looked at the grid again. It was a square. Five by five. That meant twenty-five items. She counted the bodies. One. Two. Ten. Twenty-four.

"One is missing," she said.

The blue-shirt man frowned. "We know that. The niche is empty."

"Exactly," Yejide said.

She began to circle the room. She touched the empty niche. Her fingernail scraped the stone. She returned to the filled ones. She compared them. She looked for a duplicate of the missing shape. There were none. She looked for a break in the sequence. There was a break, but it was the hole itself.

She worked harder. She tried to fit the surrounding birds into the gap mentally. She rotated the image. She matched angles. She searched the contents for the answer. She catalogued the defects she found none.

She felt the clock in her chest. She needed to finish. She needed to pass the threshold.

Her shoulders tightened. She lifted her hands. She prepared to lift a bird to force the lock.

The blue-shirt man grabbed her wrist. His grip was iron. "Stop."

"Why?"

"It will not open."

"Then we find the key."

"There is no key," he said. "You are moving the wrong parts."

Yejide pulled her hand free. She did not argue. She stood there. Her arms dropped to her sides. Her fingers curled. She held the tension there.

The room held its breath. The light was static. The birds sat in their places. They were objects. They were solid.

Yejide looked at the bench. It was low. Stone. Cold.

She sat down.

Her legs folded beneath her. She placed her palms on her knees. Her breathing slowed. She stopped looking for action. She stopped looking for the defect in the material.

She looked at the structure.

She looked at the wall not as a collection of items, but as a surface. She saw the grid. She saw the repetition. She saw the rhythm.

Five rows. Five columns.

Every other spot was full. Every spot except one.

She did not move her hands. She did not reach. She let her eyes rest on the empty niche.

In her mind, she asked a question. Not about the birds. About the gap.

*What should be here?*

She held the question. She did not answer it. She let the absence exist.

The room shifted.

It happened at the edge of vision. The light in the empty niche deepened. The shadow inside the stone rectangle sharpened. It became heavier than the shadows cast by the wood. It occupied more space than the stone allowed.

The empty niche did not recede. It expanded.

The other birds dimmed. Their grain faded. The colour washed out. The empty spot became the only thing with focus. It pulled the attention. It was the foreground. The birds were the background.

Yejide saw the shape of the void. It was not random. It had an outline. It expected a mass. It expected a form.

She spoke. She did not speak to the men. She spoke to the wall.

"There is nothing here," she said.

"No," the blue-shirt man said.

"One bird." She corrected herself. "The bird that belongs here."

She pointed at the empty space. Her finger did not shake. She felt the relief in her muscles. The tension unspooled from her neck.

The door clicked.

The mechanism engaged before she moved. The lock disengaged. The heavy wood swung inward.

The other men stood still. The woman closed her binder. The cyclist lowered his arm.

They had been working. They had been sorting. They had been counting. They had exhausted their methods.

Yejide stood up. She walked through the doorway. The concrete smell of the city replaced the smell of old wood.

Sunlight hit her face. The traffic noise rushed back in. Horns. Engines. Shouting.

Across the street, the man in the grey coat was still there. He stood under the tree. He held a small black book in his left hand. He did not look at her. He wrote in the book.

He closed it. He turned. He walked away into the crowd.

Yejide watched him go. She remembered the tree. She remembered the coat. She did not know what he had written.

Two days passed.

The argument started in the kitchen. Fadeke stood over the table. Papers covered the surface. Forms. Receipts. Pen caps rolled off the edge.

"You told me to wait," Fadeke said. "Now you say I cannot apply."

"I said to wait for the money," Yejide said. She held a plate of food. "The bank account is empty."

"I did the work," Fadeke said. She pushed a file across the table. "I filed the forms. I queued at the office. You did the rest."

"I paid the transport," Yejide said. "I bought the stamps."

"That was yesterday. I need the payment now."

Yejide put the plate down. She looked at the paper. She listed the costs. Transport. Stamp. Form fee. Late penalty. She added them in her head. She listed the deadlines. Monday. Wednesday. Friday.

She felt the anger rise in her throat. It tasted like copper. It tasted like the bus ride. She opened her mouth to speak. To defend the cost. To explain the delay.

She stopped.

She thought of the stone wall. The empty niche. The way the shadows had deepened when she stopped looking at the wood.

She looked at Fadeke. She did not look at the forms. She looked at her sister's face. She looked at the tension in Fadeke's jaw. She saw the fatigue in the shoulders.

Yejide put her hands on the table. Flat. Still.

"What do you actually need from me right now?"

She asked it quietly. No volume. No command. Just the question.

Fadeke stopped speaking. Her mouth closed. She blinked. The motion broke. She looked at Yejide. She looked past her. She looked at the pile of paper.

She exhaled. She sat on the chair. The energy drained out of her frame. The fight went out of the room.

"Just the confirmation," Fadeke said. "That it is possible."

Yejide nodded. She picked up the pen. She did not calculate the cost. She did not list the obstacles. She signed the bottom line.

Fadeke watched the ink dry. She did not smile. She breathed. The conversation changed register. It became smaller. It became manageable.

Yejide wiped the ink from her thumb. She looked at the desk. She looked at the problem. She knew the method now. It was not about the data present. It was about the shape of the hole.

The name attached to the feeling. It arrived after the closure. It was simple.

This was called asking what is missing.

The anchor stood across the street after the gate opened. He wrote in the book while the city moved around him. He did not turn his head. He recorded the data while the world continued.
